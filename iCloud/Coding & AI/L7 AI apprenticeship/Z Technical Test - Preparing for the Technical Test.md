The following notes provide additional information on error and bias in data which leads to error and/or uncertainty in the output of the analysis (i.e. the prediction).

  

**Error**

  

There are three main types of error that occur in data.

  

Gross error - human oversight while reading and recording e.g. human records 45 instead of 450 or makes a mistake in a calculation

  

Random errors - occur irregularly. Can arise due to random and unpredictable fluctuations in experimental conditions (e.g. fluctuations in temperature, vibrations, voltage supply). These are sometimes known as **precision** errors.

  

Systematic errors - occurs when an observed or calculated value deviates from the true value in a consistent way (e.g. a measuring instrument calibrated incorrectly) - sometimes known as **bias** errors.

  

- instrumental errors (faulty equipment regularly giving erroneous readings - could be calibrated wrongly or faulty),

- observational errors (a person's bias or carelessness in making observations),

- environmental (e.g. the environment consistently making a reading wrong - temperature, pressure, humidity)

  

Some literature just talks about two types of error with human error being able to fall into either category e.g., a random mistake (someone a momentarily distracted and makes a recording mistake), a systematic mistake (e.g., someone had misunderstood a task and consistently makes the same mistake in many records). Make sure your discussion of **error**, **bias,** and **uncertainty** is related to the data set you construct from the scenario which will be used as input to the analysis

**e) Explain the differences between the types of uncertainty associated with the outputs of both data collection and data analysis. Include a comparison of uncertainty in data before and after analysis.**

  

Uncertainty is the quantitative estimation of error present in data. Data scientists need to understand that there will always be some uncertainty in results obtained because of random or systematic errors that can occur in the data. There are two main types of uncertainty:

  

**Aleatory Uncertainty** - refers to the inherent uncertainty that is always present due to underlying probabilistic variability.

  

**Epistemic Uncertainty** - refers to a lack of knowledge about the (complex) system we are trying to model.

  

There are other types of uncertainty but the above two are the types discussed most often for AI and Data Science and their discussion will be sufficient for the technical test requirements.

  

**Aleatory** uncertainty occurs in the **output of** **data collection** and cannot be reduced. **Epistemic** uncertainty occurs in the output of **data analysis** as you have an imperfect model and incomplete coverage of the data. **Aleatory** uncertainty also feeds through to the analysis causing aleatory uncertainty in the **output of data analysis**. The ML algorithms used may include random elements so add to the aleatory uncertainty in the output of data analysis. So, the **output of data analysis can have both aleatory and epistemic uncertainty.**

  

Can epistemic uncertainty occur in the output of data collection? Yes, as you do not know about relevant data that you might not have collected. However, in the ML articles on this topic, epistemic uncertainty is discussed almost exclusively in the context of output of analysis. So, I suggest you do the same in your answer. Discuss aleatory uncertainty in the context of the output of data collection and epistemic uncertainty in the context of data analysis. But also state that aleatory uncertainty from the output of data collection feeds through to the outputs of data analysis so the **output of data analysis can have both aleatory and epistemic uncertainty**.

Make sure you differentiate between **aleatoric** and **epistemic** uncertainty by providing examples of aleatoric uncertainty which will be present **after data collection** in the scenario and examples of both aleatoric and epistemic **with might occur as the result of analysis.** Make sure you relate this the scenario and your planned solution. For aleatoric uncertainty use relevant data fields (features) that will be input into the analysis,

  

Aleatoric uncertainty from data collection is the result of random errors that might have occurred in the data. Even a random sample from a known distribution has aleatoric uncertainty in it because different distributions will be drawn with each sample. You can also mention that collected features might have random errors, possibly due to mistakes in human transcription/collection/input process. If a feature is collected from a machine e.g. weighing scales, you might say that weighing scales can be affected by air pressure, maintenance of mechanism, temperature, humidity the way the load is placed on it and hence give variables readings for the same load. Similar explanations can be given for other mechanical devices. Electronic devices serving data might be affected by power supply and also temperature, pressure and mechanical stress. These can cause variations in output, i.e. aleatory uncertainty.

  

Aleatoric uncertainty in the output of analysis is being uncertain about the prediction because of undetected random errors which naturally will have been fed into the analysis. A machine learning algorithm with random elements also gives rise to aleatory uncertainty in analysis if it produces different outputs each run.

  

Epistemic uncertainty refers to uncertainty regarding correctness of the model as errors may occur because not all relevant cases or features have been included in training data or because of limitations in the model. Perhaps the model is too simple or too complex. Perhaps the best set of hyper-parameter values were not utilised. A different algorithm might yield different results. Epistemic uncertainty refers to uncertainty about the prediction because of lack of knowledge. Aleatoric uncertainty cannot be reduced but can be quantified. Epistemic uncertainty can be reduced by obtaining more data or by making improvements to the model.

Note you also need to discuss quantification of uncertainty because of the underlying standard:

  

_S21: Identify and quantify different kinds of uncertainty in the outputs of data collection, experiments, and analyses_

  

Uncertainty can be quantified by using probability and statistics. A probability distribution describes the probability of occurrences of different outcomes. To quantify the uncertainty in the output of analysis, you need to get a probability distribution of outcomes. This can be obtained by running a number of tests with a different sample of test data each time. Or you could adjust the model, for instance test with various hyperparameter configurations, to obtain a probability distribution of results. You can even test with a variety of appropriate algorithms. Once you have a distribution of results, you can use probability and confidence intervals to quantity the uncertainty. For instance, you can show the mean and two standard deviations from the mean in both directions. Assuming a normal distribution, you can then say that you have 95% confidence that the outcomes lies between the bounds given by the two standard deviation measures. In a normal distribution 95% of values are within two standard deviations of the mean. The higher value indicated by the two standard deviations measure is the upper bound and the lower value is the lower bound of the 95% confidence measure.

  

Quantification is useful for communicating uncertainty to stakeholders who may need to decide on the future of the project. Interval analysis allows you to calculate probabilities of values occurring within particular ranges given particular distributions. Some machine learning packages facilitate modelling of uncertainty (e.g. Tensorflow Probability). Mention the use of averages, standard deviation, confidence levels and probability for the quantification of uncertainty. The data scientist can present results showing averages and standard deviations with probability level (confidence level) to give the stakeholders appropriate expectations of the model so that they can make appropriate decisions on future usage.

Developing an AI solution is experimental and sometime the aim of a development may not be achieved as a suitable model cannot be produced.
