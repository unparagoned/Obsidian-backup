## Professional discussion overview and assessment facts

Assessment Method 2 is the professional discussion: a one-to-one, 60-minute structured interview with an independent assessor (possible 10% extension to complete a final answer), with a minimum of 10 open questions drawn from the EPAO question bank plus assessor follow-ups. It focuses on KSBs less likely to occur naturally in the project report, presentation, or technical test, and can draw on broader workplace experience, not only the project. Answer in the first person: "I did", "I decided", "I learned", "I influenced". To pass, all AM2 pass descriptors must be met; for distinction, all pass descriptors and all AM2 distinction descriptors must be met.

The method is not underpinned by a summative portfolio — the journal is a memory aid only.

## Response structure (STAR / STARR)

Use a structured answer so each response proves competence rather than becoming a technical ramble.

- **Situation** — context and why it mattered.
- **Task** — what I was responsible for.
- **Action** — what I personally did and why.
- **Result** — business/technical outcome, evidence, learning.

For distinction-level answers, add:

- **Trade-off** — alternative options and why I chose one.
- **Risk** — what could go wrong and how I managed it.
- **Reflection** — what changed in my practice or in the organisation.

Answering approach: be concise but complete (why, how, impact, adaptation, evidence); always justify choices and explain why alternatives were rejected; reference concrete artefacts (code, dashboards, test logs, stakeholder feedback); show both technical depth and wider implications (compliance, ethics, leadership, communication); demonstrate adaptability across audiences. Distinction behaviours: critically assess and adapt practice; clearly evidence influence on working practices, decisions and solution quality; articulate commercial/ethical trade-offs and justify them in context.

Useful closing sentence: *"The key judgement was balancing technical performance with business risk, governance, cost, maintainability, and stakeholder adoption."*

### K7
"How to solve problems and evaluate software solutions via analysis of test data and results from research, feasibility, acceptance and usability testing."*

**Problem solving and evaluating software solutions via test data, research, feasibility, acceptance and usability testing**

**Situation:** iXBRL documents such as company accounts and tax computations have items tagged so a machine can extract them easily, but tax computations only had around 30% of figures tagged, so existing systems could not profile across the full data population. Extracted untagged figures could have many different free-text descriptions of what they represented.

**Task:** Categorise the various descriptions into standard taxonomy concepts so analysts could profile across the full data population.

**Action:** I researched a wide range of methods: scikit-learn classifiers (cosine similarity, Naive Bayes, Random Forest, LinearSVC), neural-network approaches (CNN/MLP/RNN), and transformer-based classifiers (BERT, SEC-BERT), plus embedding models (MPNet, e5). Since the content was accountancy text, a BERT model trained specifically on accountancy terminology (SEC-BERT) was attractive because it should understand domain terminology better. I compared models using macro-F1, accuracy, precision, recall, speed, simplicity, maintainability and explainability, using stratified train/test/holdout splits and cross-validation so I could run paired t-tests to check whether performance differences between scikit-learn models were statistically meaningful. For hyperparameter tuning across many combinations I used tools such as HalvingGridSearchCV for the scikit-learn models and Optuna for the neural/transformer models, which automated a lot of the manual comparison work I'd otherwise have had to script by hand. During testing, SEC-BERT did outperform other models such as MPNet and e5-style embeddings.

**Result:** While SEC-BERT had the best raw performance, properly training it required GPUs that would be expensive to provision and couldn't run on existing CPU architecture. SEC-BERT is also produced by a fairly niche creator rather than a well-known reputable provider, which is a security/provenance risk to bring onto the estate — directly, or because it might not behave as expected — and it has weaker explainability than simpler models. LinearSVC with TF-IDF was much faster, had good quality results, ran on existing CPU infrastructure, and had much better explainability both in terms of how the model works and via feature coefficients showing what it takes into account. It's simpler to maintain, and using it to categorise the many different descriptions of the same concept has directly helped end users exploit the extracted data.

**Reflection:** The best and most complex models don't always mean they are the best operational choice — simpler models can get very similar results while offering speed, explainability, and lower deployment risk. A lot of the manual comparison work for the scikit-learn models involved building bespoke functions to evaluate across data sizes and iterations; discovering that Optuna automated much of this for the NN/BERT work taught me to spend more time researching whether a package already does what I want before rushing to code it myself.

### K16
"Understand high-performance computer architectures and how to make effective use of these."*

**High-performance computing architectures**

**Situation:** HMRC data includes structured XML, semi-structured iXBRL/XHTML, and unstructured PDFs, arriving in large volume and varied format.

**Task:** Process the data quickly and make it easy to profile at scale.

**Action:** I use Apache Solr, a distributed document database, for fast text searching and profiling across large volumes — its server count has increased over time for more data and better speed. Hubble, the R-based extraction tool, runs on the POSIT Data Analytics Platform (DAP), but that platform wasn't large enough for the full extraction volume, so I arranged On-Demand Compute (ODC): large temporary platforms — for example 128 cores, or GPU-enabled where the work is ML-intensive — spun up for a job and shut down afterwards. Outputs are written to Oracle, a relational database, for analyst querying.

**Result:** Solr gives very fast searching and profiling; ODC's large parallel processing extracts, transforms and loads bespoke code quickly and cost-effectively, since GPU time in particular is only paid for when needed.

**Reflection:** Solr is much faster than the Oracle relational store, so I'd like to bring more of the Oracle-held data into the Solr/Apache ecosystem. Longer term, as we move to a lakehouse, the R extraction code could become a SparkR job on EMR, writing to Redshift — better integrating with other datasets while keeping lakehouse-scale performance. A weakness I've hit with ODC: some parallel-processing packages split work statically before starting, which performs poorly when documents vary widely in size — some cores end up idle while others are overloaded. Packages that split work dynamically handle this better.

### K18
"The programming languages and techniques applicable to data engineering."*

**Programming languages and techniques applicable to data engineering**

**Situation:** Company returns (XML) and iXBRL accounts (X/HTML) hold a lot of valuable data that needs to be extracted, transformed and loaded (ETL) so it can be profiled in bulk.

**Task:** Choose the right language and tooling at each stage of that pipeline.

**Action:** There's no single best language. Java moves data from the original stores into S3 buckets and extracts iXBRL/XML into Apache Solr. I built the untagged-data extraction tool in R, since R's HTML/XML parsing packages are strong and the analysts in this area already work in R. For the ML classification step, R's ML packages were less mature and less well documented than Python's (scikit-learn, TensorFlow), so I did the ML in Python and used `reticulate` to let the core R workflow call those Python functions — keeping an R-first workflow for analysts while using more mature Python ML tooling. **Result:** A mix of languages and packages transforms structured, semi-structured and unstructured data into forms the business can profile across in bulk. Core pattern: extract, transform, canonicalise, validate, load, document, monitor.

**Reflection:** Use the best tool for the job — the right language and technique depends on the systems, team, users, task, package maturity, deployment environment and maintainability, not a fixed preference.

### K19
"The principles and properties behind statistical and machine learning methods."*

**Principles behind statistical and machine learning methods**

**Situation:** Agent-level risk data needed a quantitative comparison to determine whether a specific agent posed higher risk than others.

**Task:** Test that quantitatively rather than relying on qualitative impression.

**Action:** I proposed a null hypothesis that the agent had the same risk as others. The data was two independent groups, ratio data, approximately normally distributed, with n > 30 and unknown population standard deviation. An F-test showed the variances were unequal, so Welch's t-test was the appropriate choice over Student's t-test.

**Result:** With a high 99% confidence level, I could reject the null hypothesis — the agent was shown to be higher risk than others.

**Reflection:** Statistical tests can be used more widely than I initially applied them — I might qualitatively suspect something is significantly different, but a proper test shows that quantitatively rather than by impression. More broadly: supervised classification learns a function from labelled examples to unseen cases; text is converted to numeric features via TF-IDF or embeddings; LinearSVC learns separating hyperplanes in high-dimensional sparse feature space; neural networks learn weights via loss, gradients, backpropagation and optimisation; transformers use self-attention to model token relationships; regularisation controls overfitting; macro-F1, stratification and per-class metrics manage class imbalance. ### K22
"The relationship between mathematical principles and core techniques in AI and data science within the organisational context."*

**Relationship between mathematical principles and core AI/data science techniques in the organisational context**

**Situation:** Hubble needed to classify inconsistent free-text descriptions from iXBRL accounts into standard taxonomy concepts.

**Task:** Select a classification pipeline that performed well while meeting HMRC's constraints: maintainable, built on well-established packages, runnable on existing hardware, quick enough to retrain on recent data, and explainable both technically and in terms of *why* it categorised something as it did.

**Action:** I tried different text-embedding approaches: transformer-based embeddings can group similar descriptions together even when the actual wording differs. MPNet embeddings tested significantly better than TF-IDF at a 95% confidence level, but the absolute difference was tiny (0.00003 percentage points) and MPNet was 67× slower. I also tested TF-IDF over both words and characters, and in the end chose word-level TF-IDF over character-level or embeddings because it was faster, more explainable (especially combined with the model choice), and captured the specific accountancy terminology well. I found SEC-BERT, a model pre-trained specifically on accountancy terms, which had the best raw performance — 2 percentage points better macro-F1 than every other model tested — but it's a niche model without the assurance level needed to bring onto the HMRC estate, requires GPU infrastructure and long training times, and is less explainable than other models. LinearSVC worked well with TF-IDF because TF-IDF produces a very sparse matrix, which pairs well with L1 penalty and `dual=False`, driving most feature weights to zero.

**Result:** TF-IDF with LinearSVC performed well and was much faster than the more complex alternatives. Feature coefficients increase explainability — I can see how the model works — training runs on existing CPU infrastructure, and inference is fast.

**Reflection:** The most advanced method isn't always the right choice for HMRC. Simpler models can carry real advantages — speed, explainability, lower infrastructure risk — that make them better suited to the operational context even when a more complex model scores marginally higher.

### K25
"Programming languages and modern machine learning libraries for commercially beneficial scientific analysis and simulation."*

**Programming languages and modern machine learning libraries for commercially beneficial analysis and simulation**

**Situation:** Descriptions in accounts use many different words, phrasing and stemming for the same underlying concept.

**Task:** Categorise the descriptions using the most effective available libraries.

**Action:** I tried R first, but its ML packages were less mature, so I moved to Python, which has stronger ML libraries. I started with the simplest approach — cosine similarity from scikit-learn — then moved through other scikit-learn classifiers (Naive Bayes, Random Forest, LinearSVC), then BERT (using `datasets` to pull the model from Hugging Face, with `transformers` and `torch` for fine-tuning), and finally hand-built neural networks in Keras/TensorFlow, including a CNN and a from-scratch RNN, where I had to select and configure each layer myself.

**Result:** Cosine similarity performed poorly — likely because generic embeddings don't capture the specific accountancy language used. LinearSVC did reasonably well but wasn't perfect. BERT performed well but had significant throughput problems at larger dataset sizes. Of the neural networks I built, a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) came out on top, with good performance and better scalability to more data than the sequence models.

**Reflection:** I expected the more advanced BERT/RNN-style models to have a clearer advantage; they didn't, and I was surprised an MLP came out ahead of both when weighed across all factors, including that a CNN could be genuinely useful for text-only analysis despite being designed for images. More complex isn't automatically better — even where BERT was competitive, it was too large and slow to run over all the data for a marginal, non-significant gain. This also informs the commercial benefit picture: faster analyst workflows, reusable methodology, lower manual effort, better risk identification and stronger analytical coverage — using MLflow for experiment tracking, comparison, reproducibility and model versioning discipline throughout. Important distinction for a discussion answer: describe the final deployed model (TF-IDF + LinearSVC, per K7/K22) and treat MLP/BERT/CNN/RNN as experimentation that informed that decision — not implying every prototype was production-ready.

### S19
"Use scalable infrastructures, high performance networks, infrastructure and services management and operation to generate effective business solutions."*

**Scalable infrastructures, high-performance networks, and services management to generate effective business solutions**

**Situation:** Some infrastructure needs to scale up or down with demand.

**Task:** Set infrastructure up so it scales appropriately without runaway cost.

**Action:** I initially built a database on native AWS auto-scaling services.

**Result:** These were easier to set up and back up, but turned out to be expensive even at zero usage, with opaque, hard-to-predict billing. For the smaller projects I was working on, fixed-price systems or self-hosted EC2 instances were more predictable — there's no simple, reliable way to forecast serverless cost, and it can end up costing more than a fixed database, while a fixed database is relatively easy to resize when genuinely needed. Some packages initially split the extraction work statically, which performed badly because documents vary hugely in size — some cores sat idle while others were overloaded — so I moved to packages that split work dynamically.

**Reflection:** Scalability has to be judged on both technical throughput *and* financial sustainability; the "auto-scaling by default" answer isn't automatically the right one at every project size.

### S20

**Efficient algorithms/APIs for accessing and analysing large amounts of data**

**Situation:** Hubble needs to process a large volume of iXBRL documents.

**Task:** Process that volume efficiently without saturating the main platform.

**Action:** I used parallel processing to handle many documents simultaneously, working with DevOps to set up ODC so a dedicated platform could process around 128 returns in parallel and then shut down. Rather than writing extracted output to loose files, I set up a database so it could be queried and joined efficiently.

**Result:** I could process large volumes on a separate system — often beyond what the main platform could handle — without impacting it, and storing to a database made the data far easier for analysts to query. **Reflection:** Parallel processing alone wasn't enough; it took working with DevOps to get large-enough platforms available on demand. Efficient design isn't only about algorithmic complexity — data locality, database access, batching, parallelism, and avoiding repeated manual file movement all matter.

### K8
"How to interpret organisational policies, standards and guidelines in relation to AI and data."*

**Interpreting organisational policies, standards and guidelines in relation to AI and data**

**Example 1 — challenging overly broad AI guidance.**

**Situation:** New organisational guidance on LLM/AI use came out, since these are newer technologies with significant risk. The guidance treated all machine learning as if it carried the same risk profile as generative AI/LLMs.

**Task:** Review the guidance and ensure projects complied — while avoiding unnecessary business blockage.

**Action:** I reviewed it against practical ML workflows and found it wasn't possible to follow without significant business impact: it restricted systems to LLM-oriented platforms that lacked the functionality for basic ML development, or would have been very cumbersome even where they technically could. I gave detailed feedback explaining specifically why it wasn't implementable and how it should change.

**Result:** The guidance was changed in line with my comments.

**Reflection:** AI is changing rapidly, so guidance can be outdated or not fit for purpose. It's important to critically review guidance rather than apply it blindly, and to balance protection with business need and current technical understanding.

**Example 2 — DPIA and ethical framing for the classifier.**

**Situation:** ML over business data carries inherent ethical and legal risk, especially around bias, misuse and interpretability.

**Task:** Account for those frameworks while designing and deploying the classification model.

**Action:** I completed a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) before any work began, assessed the model against GDPR and fairness considerations, and ensured human-in-the-loop validation before operational use — documenting in deployment notes that classifications are advisory, not for automated decisions.

**Result:** The DPIA surfaced risks and put mitigations in place beforehand, ensuring compliance with data protection and ethical guidelines and avoiding reputational/legal risk.

**Reflection:** Embedding ethical awareness into development up front avoids downstream problems; I'd like to build in systems that guarantee human-in-the-loop guidance is always followed, not just documented.

# Technical answer bank

For when the assessor asks "what does that mean?", "why did you use it?", or "what are the strengths and weaknesses?". Give a plain-English explanation first, then the formula.

## Quick refreshers

**Supervised learning** learns from labelled examples to predict labels for new cases — Hubble uses tagged iXBRL concepts as labels and predicts concepts for untagged descriptions.

**Unsupervised learning** finds structure without labels, e.g. clustering similar descriptions or detecting anomalies — useful for EDA, taxonomy exploration, or spotting unusual documents.

**Semi-supervised learning** combines a small amount of labelled data with a large amount of unlabelled data (self-training, label propagation, pseudo-labelling) — useful when labels are expensive or limited (image/text classification, web content tagging).

**Reinforcement learning** — an agent learns by interacting with an environment and receiving rewards/penalties, learning a policy that maximises cumulative reward (Q-learning, SARSA, Deep Q-Networks). Applications: robotics, game AI (AlphaGo), resource optimisation, automated control. Less common in enterprise data science than the other paradigms.

**Self-supervised learning** — the model generates its own labels from raw data (e.g. predicting masked words), learning useful representations without manual labelling. Underpins large language models, modern speech recognition and computer vision.

**NLP** processes text — in Hubble this includes tokenisation, normalisation, canonicalisation, TF-IDF vectorisation, embeddings and text classification.

**Data mining** discovers hidden patterns, trends and relationships in large datasets (clustering, association rule mining, anomaly detection) to support decisions — fraud detection, customer behaviour, market-basket analysis, disease patterns. In Hubble: frequency analysis, ambiguity analysis, concept distribution, description overlap, model error patterns.

**Data quality dimensions:** accuracy, completeness, consistency, validity, timeliness, uniqueness, lineage, relevance. Hubble examples: canonicalising names/dates/numbers improved consistency; removing PII/noise improved relevance and privacy; tracking document source and taxonomy supports lineage; per-class performance checks reveal where output isn't reliable enough.

## Overfitting, bias-variance and imbalance

**Overfitting:** the model learns training data too well, including noise — high training accuracy, low test accuracy. Addressed by regularisation, more data, a simpler model, dropout, cross-validation. **Underfitting:** the model is too simple to capture the pattern — low accuracy on both training and test. Addressed by a more complex model, better features, less regularisation.

**Bias–variance trade-off:** bias is error from oversimplifying assumptions (underfitting); variance is error from sensitivity to small fluctuations in training data (overfitting). The goal is minimising total error (bias² + variance + irreducible noise).

**Cross-validation:** a single train/test split is unreliable. K-fold CV splits data into k folds, trains on k−1 and validates on the remaining fold, repeating k times for a more robust generalisation estimate. **Stratified CV** keeps class proportions consistent across folds — essential for imbalanced data such as Hubble's taxonomy.

**Handling imbalanced data:** when one class vastly outnumbers another, accuracy is misleading (a model that always predicts the majority class can score 99%+ while being useless). Solutions: **SMOTE** (synthetic minority oversampling — generates synthetic minority examples by interpolation), **class weighting** (penalise minority-class errors more heavily), **undersampling** the majority class, and using precision/recall/F1/AUC-ROC rather than raw accuracy.

## Confusion matrix and classification metrics

For one class treated as positive: **True Positive (TP)** — predicted the class, correct. **False Positive (FP)** — predicted the class, true class was different (Type I error). **False Negative (FN)** — true class was this class, model missed it (Type II error — often the more dangerous error in medical/safety contexts). **True Negative (TN)** — correctly predicted something else. In multi-class problems like iXBRL taxonomy classification, calculate these per class by treating that class as positive and all others as negative.

**Accuracy** = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN). Overall correctness; simple to explain, but misleading under class imbalance — a model can score well by nailing common classes while failing rare but important ones. *Hubble answer:* "I monitored accuracy but didn't use it as the main selection metric, because the taxonomy labels were highly imbalanced — accuracy would reward doing well on common concepts even while failing rare ones."

**Precision** = TP / (TP + FP). Of everything predicted positive, how much was right — use when false positives are costly (e.g. triggering unnecessary analyst review). *Hubble answer:* "Precision tells analysts how much they can trust predictions for a concept; low precision means the model over-predicts it and analysts should review those cases more carefully."

**Recall (sensitivity)** = TP / (TP + FN). Of all real positives, how many were found — use when false negatives are costly (security, medical screening, missing a rare but important financial concept). *Hubble answer:* "Recall matters where the business wants to avoid missing relevant concepts, balanced against precision so analysts aren't overwhelmed with false positives."

**F1 score** = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall). Harmonic mean of precision and recall — penalises extreme imbalance (very high precision with near-zero recall still yields a low F1, not 50%). Use when both false positives and false negatives matter.

**Specificity** = TN / (TN + FP). True negative rate — important in medical/risk domains.

**Macro-F1** = mean of per-class F1 across all K classes, each class weighted equally regardless of size. Prevents common classes from dominating the metric and forces attention across the whole taxonomy, not just the largest labels — this is why it fits Hubble, where hundreds of taxonomy concepts have a long tail of rare classes. Weakness: can look harsh with very little data per rare class, and needs support counts alongside it. *Hubble answer:* "I used macro-F1 because Hubble had hundreds of taxonomy concepts with a long tail of rare classes. Accuracy would mostly tell me about common concepts; macro-F1 told me whether the model worked across the taxonomy."

**Micro-F1** aggregates TP/FP/FN across all classes before computing F1 — dominated by frequent classes. **Weighted-F1** weights each class's F1 by its support (instance count) — more balanced than accuracy but still favours common classes. *Hubble answer:* "I'd report micro or weighted F1 as secondary metrics, but macro-F1 was better for model selection because rare taxonomy concepts shouldn't disappear from the evaluation."

**ROC curve & AUC:** ROC plots recall against false-positive rate across thresholds; AUC (area under the curve) is the probability a classifier ranks a random positive above a random negative — 1.0 is perfect, 0.5 is random guessing. **Precision–Recall (PR) curve** is the better alternative to ROC for highly imbalanced data — average precision (area under the PR curve) is more informative than AUC-ROC when the positive class is rare. **Balanced accuracy** = (sensitivity + specificity) / 2, correcting for class imbalance by averaging recall per class.

**Metric selection cheat sheet:**

| Problem type | Primary metric | Secondary metrics | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Balanced classification | Accuracy | F1 (weighted), confusion matrix | Fine when classes are roughly equal |
| Imbalanced classification | Macro-F1 / AUC-ROC | Precision, recall per class | Avoid accuracy alone |
| Multi-class, all classes matter (Hubble) | Macro-F1 | Confusion matrix, per-class precision/recall | Equal weight to rare classes |
| Ranking / probability output | AUC-ROC | Average precision (PR-AUC) | Threshold-independent |

## Regression evaluation metrics

**MAE (Mean Absolute Error)** = mean(|y − ŷ|). Average absolute error; easy to interpret, less sensitive to outliers. Prefer over RMSE when data quality is poor.

**MSE (Mean Squared Error)** = mean((y − ŷ)²). Squares errors so larger ones are penalised more; differentiable, widely used in optimisation, but the units are squared.

**RMSE** = √MSE. Same units as the target, penalises large errors strongly — use when large errors are especially costly.

**R² (coefficient of determination)** = 1 − (SS_res / SS_tot), where SS_res is the residual (unexplained) sum of squares and SS_tot is the total sum of squares around the mean. Measures the proportion of variance explained by the model (0 to 1, can be negative for a poor fit) — e.g. R² = 0.85 means 85% of variance is explained. Doesn't show prediction accuracy directly. **Adjusted R²** penalises unnecessary features, better for multiple regression.

**Median absolute error** is even more robust than MAE to bad data — ignores extreme outliers from data issues, a strong answer when asked how to evaluate under poor data quality.

## Clustering, dimensionality reduction and other core algorithms

**K-means clustering.** Objective: minimise Σ‖x − centroid‖² across clusters. Partitions data into K clusters by nearest centroid, iterating: assign points to the nearest centroid, recompute centroids as the mean of assigned points, repeat until convergence. Choosing K: elbow method (inertia vs K) or silhouette score. **K-means++** improves on random initialisation (plain K-means is sensitive to starting centroids). **K-medoids** uses an actual data point as the cluster centre rather than the mean — more robust to outliers, heavier to compute; choose it when outliers are a real concern. Strengths: simple, fast, scalable. Weaknesses: must choose K, sensitive to outliers/initial centroids, assumes roughly spherical, similarly-sized clusters. *Hubble angle:* "K-means could help explore groups of descriptions, but because Hubble had labelled tagged concepts, supervised learning was more appropriate for the final classification task."

**Hierarchical clustering** builds a dendrogram bottom-up (agglomerative) or top-down (divisive), using a linkage rule — single (nearest neighbour), complete (furthest neighbour), average, or Ward's method (minimises within-cluster variance, often the most compact result). No need to pre-specify K, and the dendrogram shows relationships at every scale, but it's computationally expensive (O(n²)–O(n³)) and merges can't be revised once made.

**DBSCAN** groups points in dense regions and labels sparse points as noise — doesn't need a predefined number of clusters, and handles irregular shapes and outliers well.

**Clustering metrics:** **silhouette score** (−1 to 1) measures how similar a point is to its own cluster vs other clusters — near 1 is well-clustered, near 0 is on a boundary, negative suggests the wrong cluster. **Adjusted Rand Index (ARI)** compares a clustering to a ground-truth labelling, adjusted for chance — 1 is perfect, 0 is random.

**PCA.** Reduces dimensionality by finding orthogonal directions (principal components) that maximise variance, via eigen-decomposition/SVD of the covariance matrix, then projecting data onto the top components ranked by eigenvalue (explained variance). Strengths: reduces dimensionality/noise, removes correlated features, speeds downstream modelling. Weaknesses: components aren't directly interpretable (they're linear combinations of the originals), it's a linear method, and it's sensitive to feature scale, so standardise first.

**Apriori algorithm** finds frequent itemsets and generates association rules for market-basket-style analysis. Support(A) = transactions containing A / total transactions. Confidence(A→B) = Support(A∩B) / Support(A). Lift(A→B) = Confidence(A→B) / Support(B). Builds up from frequent 1-itemsets to larger itemsets, discarding anything below minimum support at each step.

## Classic supervised algorithms

**Naive Bayes.** P(class | features) = P(features | class) × P(class) / P(features), with the "naive" assumption that features are conditionally independent given the class, so P(features|class) factorises into the product of each P(feature_i|class). Fast, works well for text, a good baseline — but the independence assumption is unrealistic, calibration is often poor, and it can't learn feature interactions. Variants: Gaussian (continuous features), Multinomial (word counts — the usual text-classification variant), Bernoulli (binary features). Generative model (models the data distribution per class), contrasted with logistic regression's discriminative approach (learns the boundary directly) — logistic regression tends to win with more data; Naive Bayes can do better with very little data. *Hubble answer:* "Naive Bayes was useful as a simple baseline, but the final model needed stronger performance and better handling of the taxonomy classes."

**Logistic regression.** p = 1 / (1 + e^−z), z = b + w₁x₁ + … + wₙxₙ. Predicts class probability via the sigmoid, thresholded (typically at 0.5) to classify — contrast with linear regression, which predicts a continuous value directly. Strengths: interpretable coefficients, fast, strong baseline, probabilistic output. Weaknesses: linear decision boundary unless features are engineered; can underfit complex relationships.

**Linear regression.** y = β₀ + β₁x₁ + … + βₚxₚ + ε, fit by minimising the sum of squared errors (least squares) — a convex problem with a single global minimum, solvable via the normal equation (β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy) or gradient descent for large datasets. Assumptions: linearity, independent errors, constant variance (homoscedasticity), normally distributed errors (for inference).

**Decision tree.** Recursively splits on the feature/threshold that most reduces impurity. **Gini impurity** = 1 − Σp_k² (lower is purer). **Entropy** = −Σp_k·log₂(p_k). **Information gain** = entropy(parent) − weighted average entropy(children); the split with highest gain is chosen. Regression trees split to minimise MSE instead. Algorithms: **ID3** (information gain, categorical only, no pruning), **C4.5** (handles continuous values and missing data, post-pruning), **CART** (binary splits, Gini for classification/MSE for regression — basis for Random Forest and Gradient Boosting). Strengths: easy to explain, handles non-linearity, little preprocessing. Weaknesses: overfits easily, unstable (small data changes → different tree), less suited to very high-dimensional sparse text than linear models.

**Random Forest.** Ensemble of decision trees trained via **bagging** (bootstrap sampling with replacement) plus **random feature selection** at each split, which decorrelates the trees. Prediction: majority vote (classification) or average (regression) across trees. Lower variance than a single tree because averaging over T less-correlated trees reduces variance roughly as Var ≈ ρσ² + ((1−ρ)/T)σ², where ρ is inter-tree correlation. Strengths: reduces variance/overfitting vs one tree, handles non-linearity, gives feature importance. Weaknesses: less interpretable, heavier to train/predict, can be less efficient than LinearSVC for sparse text.

**Gradient boosting (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost).** Builds trees sequentially, each correcting the errors of the previous one, minimising a loss function via gradient descent in function space — contrast with Random Forest's parallel, independent trees. Typically higher accuracy than Random Forest but more prone to overfitting without regularisation, and more hyperparameters to tune. LightGBM is faster on very large datasets; CatBoost handles categorical features natively. Use cases: tabular data, fraud detection, click-through-rate prediction, credit scoring.

**K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN).** Non-parametric, lazy learner — classifies a new point by the majority class among its K nearest training examples by distance. Simple, no training phase, naturally multi-class — but expensive at prediction time (scans all training data), sensitive to feature scale (needs normalisation) and suffers the curse of dimensionality in high-dimensional data. K is chosen via cross-validation (too small overfits, too large underfits). Compared with Naive Bayes: KNN makes no probabilistic assumptions but is computationally heavy and degrades badly with dimensionality; Naive Bayes is faster and handles high dimensions better, at the cost of the independence assumption.

## TF-IDF, n-grams and text classifiers

**TF-IDF.** TF(t,d) = count of term t in document d / total terms in d. IDF(t) = log(N / df(t)), N = total documents, df(t) = documents containing t. TF-IDF(t,d) = TF × IDF. Common words score low; rare, discriminative terms score high. Strengths: fast, CPU-friendly, interpretable (inspect important words/n-grams), works well with sparse linear models like LinearSVC, and suits domain-specific text where particular words/phrases carry strong signal. Weaknesses: doesn't understand word order beyond the chosen n-gram window, no real synonym/context understanding, vocabulary can drift as terminology changes, and rare misspellings create noisy features. *Hubble answer:* "TF-IDF suited Hubble because many financial descriptions are short and domain-specific — accounting terms and phrases are strong signals — and its interpretability let me explain which terms drove classifications."

**N-grams:** unigram ("interest"), bigram ("interest receivable"), trigram ("interest receivable income"). Capture phrase-level meaning that unigrams miss (useful where Hubble concepts depend on multi-word phrases), at the cost of higher feature count/sparsity and overfitting risk on rare phrases without regularisation.

**Word embeddings (Word2Vec, MPNet, e5, BERT-style):** dense vectors capturing semantic similarity, so related phrases group together even with different wording. Weaknesses: generic embeddings may miss specialist accountancy meaning, less interpretable, often slower than TF-IDF. TF-IDF frequently outperforms embeddings on domain-specific tasks with limited training data and distinctive vocabulary — embeddings need enough data to learn that domain language. *Hubble answer:* "I tested MPNet/e5-style embeddings — they capture semantic similarity, but the practical gain was too small to justify the speed and explainability cost."

**LinearSVC / linear SVM.** Decision function: score_c(x) = w_c·x + b_c, predicted class = argmax_c(score_c(x)); trained via a hinge-loss objective: minimise ½‖w‖² + C·Σmax(0, 1 − y_i(w·x_i + b)). Finds the maximum-margin separating hyperplane; LinearSVC is optimised for large, sparse datasets, especially text. Strengths: performs well with TF-IDF, fast CPU inference, handles high-dimensional sparse features, coefficients show which terms push toward a class, easier to govern/maintain than a large transformer. Weaknesses: linear boundary may miss complex non-linear relationships, not naturally probabilistic, can struggle where synonym/context understanding is needed, and hyperparameters (C, penalty, class weights) matter. *Hubble answer:* "LinearSVC was the best operational fit — it worked well with sparse TF-IDF features, had strong macro-F1, ran quickly on CPU, and was far easier to explain and maintain than a transformer."

**Kernel trick / non-linear SVM.** A kernel (RBF, polynomial) implicitly maps data into a higher-dimensional space so a linear boundary there corresponds to a non-linear one in the original space. Powerful for complex boundaries, but slower and less scalable than LinearSVC, with kernel/gamma choices that can over- or under-fit — less suitable for very large sparse text datasets. *Hubble answer:* "I focused on LinearSVC rather than kernel SVM because Hubble had high-dimensional sparse text features and needed scalable training and fast inference." **C parameter:** controls the margin–error trade-off — small C gives a wider margin and tolerates more misclassification (soft margin); large C gives a narrower margin with fewer errors (harder margin, more overfitting risk).

**Regularisation.** L1 (Lasso): loss + λΣ|w_j| — drives some coefficients to exactly zero, acting as feature selection, useful given TF-IDF's large sparse feature space. L2 (Ridge): loss + λΣw_j² — shrinks all coefficients smoothly, rarely to zero. Elastic Net combines both. Too much regularisation underfits; too little overfits. *Hubble answer:* "L1 regularisation mattered because TF-IDF creates many sparse features — it set unhelpful features to zero, improving simplicity and interpretability."

**Class weighting.** Typically weight_k = N / (K × n_k), increasing the penalty for errors on rare classes. Easy to implement in scikit-learn, helps minority classes influence training, but can reduce performance on common classes and over-sensitise the model to noisy rare labels. *Hubble answer:* "I used class weighting because the taxonomy had dominant classes and a long tail — it stopped the model optimising only for common concepts."

## Neural networks

A neural network is a layered function approximator: an input layer of features, one or more hidden layers doing non-linear transformations, and an output layer producing the prediction.

**Inside a neuron:** weighted sum z = w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + … + wₙxₙ + b, then a non-linear **activation** a = f(z). Weights determine each input's importance; bias shifts the function. The whole network represents a parameterised function ŷ = f(x; W, b); training means finding the weights and biases that minimise a **loss function** — MSE for regression (L = mean((y−ŷ)²)) or cross-entropy for classification (L = −Σy·log(ŷ)).

**Gradients and backpropagation.** A gradient ∂L/∂w is the partial derivative of the loss with respect to a parameter — it gives the direction and size of the change needed. Backpropagation applies the chain rule to compute these efficiently layer by layer: ∂L/∂w = (∂L/∂a)·(∂a/∂z)·(∂z/∂w), where ∂a/∂z is the activation's derivative and ∂z/∂w = x. This reuses intermediate derivatives so it scales to deep networks — in short, backprop calculates how much each weight contributed to the final error.

**Gradient descent.** Weights update as w_new = w_old − η·∂L/∂w, moving opposite the gradient (downhill), where η is the learning rate. Variants: batch (full dataset), stochastic/SGD (one sample at a time), mini-batch (small batches — most common in practice). Too large a learning rate causes divergence; too small is slow; local minima/saddle points are addressed with momentum or the Adam optimiser. Training loop: forward pass → compute loss → backpropagate gradients → update weights via gradient descent → repeat until convergence.

**Activation functions.** **Sigmoid** σ(z) = 1/(1+e^−z), output (0,1), smooth and interpretable as a probability, but suffers vanishing gradients for large |z| (σ'(z) ≈ 0), slowing deep-network training — typically used at the output layer for binary classification. **Tanh** = (e^z − e^−z)/(e^z + e^−z), output (−1,1), zero-centred (better than sigmoid) with stronger gradients, but still suffers vanishing gradients. **ReLU** = max(0,z), output [0,∞), gradient is 1 for z>0 and 0 for z≤0 — simple, fast, and avoids vanishing gradients for active neurons, which is why it's the default for hidden layers in deep networks; its weakness is the **dying ReLU problem**, where a neuron whose weights keep z≤0 stops learning entirely (gradient permanently zero). Variants: **Leaky ReLU** (f(z)=max(0.01z, z), allows a small gradient when z<0), **Parametric ReLU/PReLU** (learnable negative slope), **ELU** (smooth negative values). **Softmax** (output layer, multi-class): Softmax(z_i) = e^{z_i} / Σ_j e^{z_j} — exponentiates each logit then normalises by the sum, giving a probability distribution across classes that sums to 1 and emphasises the largest values; used with cross-entropy loss for multi-class training. Worked example: logits [2, 1, 0.1] → exponentials [7.39, 2.72, 1.11] → sum 11.21 → softmax output ≈ [0.66, 0.24, 0.10].

| Activation | Range | Zero-centred | Vanishing gradient | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sigmoid | (0, 1) | No | Yes | Binary output layer |
| Tanh | (−1, 1) | Yes | Yes (less severe) | Hidden layers (older nets) |
| ReLU | [0, ∞) | No | Rare (positive z) | Hidden layers (default) |
| Softmax | (0, 1), sums to 1 | — | — | Multi-class output layer |

*Hubble answer:* "I built neural-network prototypes to test whether non-linear models would improve classification. They were useful experimentally, but the final operational decision also had to weigh explainability, speed, governance and maintainability — an MLP can learn complex non-linear interactions that LinearSVC can't, but it needs more data, more tuning, and is a black box next to LinearSVC's interpretable coefficients."

**CNN for text.** Convolutional filters slide over the sequence, learning local patterns (phrase-like features), pooling layers downsample feature maps (max pooling keeps the largest value per region), and fully connected layers do the final classification. Faster than recurrent models and surprisingly effective for text despite being designed for images, but less interpretable than TF-IDF coefficients.

**RNN and LSTM.** RNNs process sequences step-by-step, carrying a hidden state forward — but standard RNNs suffer vanishing gradients over long sequences, making long-range dependencies hard to learn. **LSTM** adds gating (input, forget, output gates) to selectively remember or forget information, mitigating that. Used for time series, speech, language modelling, sequence tasks — but sequential processing makes both slower to train than transformers, and both were often unnecessary for Hubble's short financial descriptions. Transformers have largely replaced RNN/LSTM for most NLP tasks because self-attention is parallelisable and captures long-range dependencies more directly.

**Transformers and BERT.** Self-attention: Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ/√d_k)·V — every token can attend to every other token in the sequence at once, capturing long-range dependencies without recurrence (Vaswani et al., 2017). **BERT** uses only the encoder stack and is bidirectional (reads context from both directions, unlike GPT's left-to-right). BERT-Base: 12 layers, 768 dimensions, 12 attention heads; BERT-Large: 24 layers, 1024 dimensions, 16 heads. Pre-training tasks: **Masked Language Modelling** (15% of tokens masked — 80% replaced with `[MASK]`, 10% with a random token, 10% left unchanged — forcing bidirectional understanding) and **Next Sentence Prediction** (do two sentences naturally follow each other). Special tokens: `[CLS]` (its final hidden state feeds classification), `[SEP]` (separates sentence pairs), `[MASK]` (used in MLM pre-training). Input embeddings combine token + segment + positional embeddings. Tokenisation uses WordPiece, splitting rare words into subwords (e.g. "playing" → "play", "##ing"). **RoBERTa** trains longer on more data, drops NSP, and uses dynamic masking and byte-pair encoding — it consistently outperforms BERT downstream. **SEC-BERT** is domain-pretrained on financial/accounting text, improving specialist terminology understanding but carrying provenance, explainability and infrastructure risk (see K7, K22). Strengths generally: strong language understanding, handles context better than TF-IDF, domain models can understand specialist terms well. Weaknesses: large and slow, usually needs GPU fine-tuning, harder to explain, supply-chain/provenance risk for niche models, more expensive to maintain and deploy. *Hubble answer:* "SEC-BERT was technically attractive — trained on financial language — but the operational trade-off was poor: GPU requirement, slower training/inference, weaker explainability, and provenance concerns. The extra complexity didn't justify the marginal performance difference for HMRC."

## Hyperparameter tuning

Hyperparameters (learning rate, tree depth, number of trees, kernel type, regularisation strength) are set *before* training and control how the model learns, as opposed to model parameters (weights), which are learned automatically. Tuning process: choose the hyperparameters to tune, define value ranges, train across combinations, evaluate on validation data, select the best. Techniques: **grid search** (tries every combination — thorough but expensive), **random search** (samples combinations — faster, often nearly as good), **Bayesian optimisation** (e.g. Optuna — uses prior results to choose smarter combinations next, efficient for complex models). I use cross-validation during tuning to check the chosen configuration generalises rather than just fitting one split. In practice: HalvingGridSearchCV for the scikit-learn models, Optuna for the neural/transformer models (see K7).

## Statistical tests

**Cross-validation (K-fold):** split data into k folds; for each, train on k−1 folds and validate on the remaining one; average the validation scores. **Stratified** CV keeps class proportions consistent per fold — important for imbalanced data. Reduces dependence on a single lucky/unlucky split, but is more expensive and requires that any data-derived preprocessing be fitted *inside* each training fold to avoid leakage.

**Paired t-test (model comparison):** t = mean(d) / (sd(d)/√n), where d is the paired score difference between two models on the same folds/runs. Controls for split-to-split variation since both models see the same partitions. Assumes roughly normal paired differences; statistical significance ≠ practical significance — a tiny, significant improvement may not be worth extra cost. *Hubble answer:* "Where a model had a tiny metric improvement, I considered whether it was practically meaningful — a very small gain wasn't worth a 67× slowdown or a GPU dependency" (the MPNet-vs-TF-IDF comparison, K22).

**Welch's t-test:** t = (mean₁ − mean₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂). Compares two independent group means when variances are unequal (checked via an F-test) — safer than Student's t-test in that case, though it still assumes reasonably normal data or large enough samples, and is sensitive to outliers. *Journal answer:* "For the agent risk comparison, I used Welch's t-test after an F-test indicated unequal variances — that let me test whether the apparent difference was statistically meaningful rather than relying on qualitative judgement alone" (K19).

**Z-test:** z = (sample mean − population mean) / (σ/√n), or for two proportions, z = (p₁ − p₂) / √(p(1−p)(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)). Compares means/proportions using the normal distribution — needs a large sample (n ≥ 30, generally) and known population variance, or adequate counts for proportions. Simple and powerful at scale, but not appropriate with unknown variance and small samples. *Example:* a 2-proportion Z-test comparing SharePoint vs OneDrive external-sharing rates in September (63/780 = 8% vs 44/2205 = 2%, overall pooled proportion 3.6%) gave Z ≈ 0.00032, far below the 5%-alpha critical value of 1.96 — so the null hypothesis (proportions are the same) was accepted, i.e. no significant difference was found between the two services' external-sharing behaviour that month.

**Chi-square (χ²) test:** χ² = Σ((observed − expected)² / expected), with degrees of freedom = categories − 1; p < 0.05 typically indicates a significant difference. Tests whether categorical variables are associated, or whether observed frequencies differ from expected ones (independence test, goodness-of-fit test) — e.g. whether incident priority is related to resolution outcome. Needs adequate expected counts per category, and shows association, not causation.

**ANOVA (Analysis of Variance):** compares means across three or more groups (one-way: one factor; two-way: two factors) — e.g. comparing mean downtime across three data centres. Avoids the inflated error rate of running many pairwise t-tests.

**Quick recall:** t-test/Z-test compare means (small vs large samples); chi-square tests relationships between categories; ANOVA compares means across 3+ groups.

## Model drift and monitoring

**Types of drift.** **Data drift** (input drift): the distribution of input features shifts vs training data (e.g. traffic patterns change after a system upgrade). **Concept drift**: the relationship between inputs and outputs changes even when inputs look similar (e.g. incidents resolve faster after automation, so old predictors lose relevance). **Prediction drift** (output drift): predictions shift abnormally even before labels arrive (e.g. a sudden spike in high-priority predictions) — useful when ground-truth labels are delayed. **Label/taxonomy drift** and **extraction drift** are Hubble-specific variants: taxonomy or tagging practice changes, or upstream HTML/iXBRL structure changes.

**Monitoring data drift:** compare training vs production distributions (mean/median, variance, histograms, percentiles) using statistical tests — **KS test** (Kolmogorov–Smirnov, continuous features), **chi-square test** (categorical features, formula above), or the **Population Stability Index (PSI)**, the most common industry choice, which works for both numeric and categorical features.

**PSI, step by step:** bin the feature the same way for training and production (e.g. deciles); for each bin i compute Expected_i (% of training data) and Actual_i (% of production data); PSI = Σ(Actual_i − Expected_i) × ln(Actual_i / Expected_i). Interpretation: PSI < 0.10 → no drift; 0.10–0.25 → moderate drift; > 0.25 → significant drift. *One-liner:* "PSI quantifies feature-distribution shift by comparing binned percentages between training and production data."

**PSI vs chi-square:** PSI works for numeric and categorical data, gives a threshold-based magnitude and is easy to explain; chi-square is categorical-only, gives statistical significance (a p-value) rather than magnitude. Use PSI for continuous monitoring with simple threshold alerts when labels are unavailable; use chi-square when you need statistical significance on categorical frequencies.

**Monitoring concept drift:** track performance metrics over time on recent labelled data (classification: accuracy/precision/recall/F1; regression: RMSE/MAE) using a sliding window (e.g. last 7 days vs the training-period baseline) — a sudden metric drop signals concept drift.

**Retraining strategy once drift is detected:** confirm the drift type (data vs concept vs prediction), collect fresh labelled data, retrain, re-validate, redeploy, and reset monitoring baselines. *Hubble answer:* "If Hubble went wrong, I'd first contain the risk because outputs are advisory. Then I'd check extraction, taxonomy changes, data drift, prediction distributions, holdout macro-F1, per-class errors and analyst feedback before retraining or rolling back."

## KPIs and accuracy under poor data quality

Algorithm-accuracy KPIs should match the problem type: **classification** — F1, precision, recall, AUC-ROC, specificity, tracked instead of raw accuracy on imbalanced data. **Regression** — MAE, RMSE, R², Map. In production, also track **stability** (accuracy/RMSE trend over rolling windows, e.g. last 7 vs last 30 days), **drift-aware accuracy** (accuracy before/after drift detection; the drop-% that should trigger retraining), and **threshold-based governance KPIs** (e.g. accuracy drop > 5–10%, F1 drop > 0.05, AUC drop below 0.7 triggering an alert or retrain).

**When data quality is poor**, raw accuracy, R² alone, and precision/recall without context all become misleading — "garbage in, garbage out" masked behind a plausible-looking number. Prefer more robust signals instead: **MAE or median absolute error** (less sensitive to noise/outliers than RMSE); **stability trends** (week-on-week or month-on-month metric variation); **drift KPIs** (PSI, KS-test, feature missing-rate); and **confidence/coverage KPIs** (% of predictions made above a confidence threshold; the low-confidence prediction rate) — low coverage or a high low-confidence rate signals unreliable input data rather than a bad model. Business-safe KPIs as a backstop: % of high-risk predictions manually overridden, % of automated decisions rolled back, cost of wrong predictions, SLA breach rate attributable to model output.

## Data science lifecycle and MLOps

**CRISP-DM:** business understanding → data understanding → data preparation → modelling → evaluation → deployment. **SEMMA:** sample → explore → modify → model → assess. The fuller operational pipeline breaks into: problem definition (with success KPIs) → data collection → EDA (missingness, outliers, distributions, correlations) → preprocessing/cleaning (handle missing values, duplicates, outliers, encoding, scaling) → feature engineering/selection → train/test split (e.g. 70–80/20–30, to evaluate on unseen data and guard against overfitting) → model selection → training (gradient descent/backprop) → evaluation (metrics matched to problem type) → hyperparameter tuning → deployment (APIs, batch/real-time integration) → monitoring and retraining as real-world data changes.

Hubble mapping: business understanding = untagged iXBRL limits profiling/risk analysis; data understanding = EDA on descriptions, concepts, imbalance, ambiguous labels, taxonomy coverage; data preparation = extraction, cleaning, PII canonicalisation, label construction; modelling = compare baselines, LinearSVC, neural networks, embeddings, transformers; evaluation = macro-F1, accuracy, precision, recall, confusion matrices, speed, cost, explainability; deployment = R/Python integration, ODC batch processing, Oracle outputs, analyst guidance.

**DataOps principles:** collaboration, automation, reuse, analytics-as-code, testing, monitoring, short cycles, data-driven improvement.

**MLOps principles:** reproducibility (retrain an old model, get comparable results), accountability (trace production output back to code/data/model/parameters), collaboration (versioned work, async review), continuous testing through the lifecycle, continuous monitoring (drift, degradation), reusable infrastructure (avoid rebuilding every project from scratch). Hubble application: GitLab issues/templates/epics/milestones, README and docs, unit/integration tests, MLflow experiment tracking, versioned notebooks/scripts, a fixed holdout set with per-class metrics, and human-in-the-loop analyst feedback.

## Data architecture options

**Relational database (Oracle):** strong structured querying, SQL, governance, joins, mature access control; weaker for text search or exploring semi-structured documents. Hubble uses it to store structured extracted outputs for analysts.

**Apache Solr / document search:** fast distributed text search and document profiling; not a relational analytics warehouse. Useful for fast searching across document-like data.

**Data lake:** stores structured, semi-structured and unstructured data in native format — scalable and cheap for raw data, but can become a "data swamp" without governance, metadata and quality controls.

**Data lakehouse:** combines lake flexibility with warehouse-style governance, schema enforcement, transactions, BI support, open formats, and decoupled storage/compute. Hubble's future direction: R/SparkR-style processing on EMR, writing to Redshift/lakehouse-style storage.

**Data fabric:** a central layer over distributed data using metadata, semantic knowledge graphs and embedded automation/ML — powerful but complex to implement and governance-heavy.

## Legal and governance quick answers

**UK GDPR principles:** lawfulness, fairness and transparency; purpose limitation; data minimisation; accuracy; storage limitation; integrity and confidentiality; accountability. (These map closely to the older OECD data-protection principles: collection limitation, data quality, purpose specification, use limitation, security safeguards, openness, individual participation, accountability.)

**Lawful bases:** consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, legitimate interests.

**Special category data:** race/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious/philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data, health, sex life, sexual orientation.

**Equality Act protected characteristics:** age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage/civil partnership, pregnancy/maternity, race, religion/belief, sex, sexual orientation.

**Article 22 (UK GDPR):** restricts solely automated decisions with legal or similarly significant effects unless a valid condition applies — this is why Hubble's outputs are documented as advisory with a human in the loop, not an automated decision.

**DPIA:** required where processing is likely to be high risk. Hubble's relevance: company accounts/tax documents can include names, references and sensitive context, so a DPIA and data minimisation were appropriate before work began (see S12/B3).

**EU AI Act:** risk-based approach — unacceptable risk banned, high risk regulated, limited risk carries transparency duties, minimal risk is free to use. Not the primary UK control for HMRC, but its principles (documentation, human oversight, robustness, risk assessment) are a useful reference point.

## Bonus: generic frameworks for open-ended questions

Three condensed frameworks for questions phrased generically rather than tied to Hubble specifically — use them as structure, then anchor the answer in the Hubble evidence above.

**"How do you select the right technique for a business problem?"** Start with the business decision and measurable KPIs, not the model. Check whether ML is even needed (rules/automation may be safer and cheaper; BI may be enough if no prediction/decision is required). Map the question to a task type (regression, classification, clustering, anomaly detection, recommendation). Assess data readiness — availability, quality, label quality, leakage risk, freshness — since this often decides the technique more than anything else. Always build a fast, interpretable baseline first to confirm feasibility and set a performance floor. Choose the model via a decision matrix against interpretability, performance, latency and governance needs — the simplest model that meets all of them, not the most powerful one available. Validate with metrics aligned to business risk, and treat MLOps/monitoring/retraining as part of the solution, not an afterthought.

**"How do you disseminate AI and data science practice?"** Standardise (common methodologies, templates, documentation); enable (communities of practice, internal wikis, role-based training); provide reusable assets (pipelines, feature libraries, prebuilt tools); embed governance (validation, bias/fairness checks, audit trails); demonstrate value through real pilots rather than theory; make results visible via dashboards; and foster a data-driven culture with senior sponsorship.

**"How do you track trends in the AI landscape?"** Combine research/publication tracking (arXiv, major labs, analyst reports), vendor/ecosystem monitoring (platform updates, open-source tooling), enterprise adoption-pattern analysis, community engagement (forums, conferences), and hands-on validation through PoCs — then filter hype from real value by assessing scalability, cost/benefit and governance fit before recommending adoption.

## Will AI change jobs?

Balanced answer: AI will remove *some* jobs, but more often it changes jobs by automating tasks within a role rather than eliminating whole occupations — most roles combine routine tasks (easy to automate), judgement/context tasks (harder), and accountability/people-facing tasks (often required by policy or compliance). Independent research broadly agrees on scale, if not precise numbers: the WEF projects large churn by 2030 (170m roles created, 92m displaced); the IMF estimates around 40% of global employment is exposed to AI (higher — around 60% — in advanced economies); the ILO finds roughly one in four workers in occupations with some genAI exposure, with clerical work particularly exposed. Higher-risk work tends to be high-volume, standardised, digital, and low on nuanced accountability; lower-risk work involves complex human interaction, unstructured physical environments, or high-stakes regulated decisions. The practical framing for a discussion answer: **displacement** happens where enough tasks are automated that fewer people are needed; **augmentation** happens where AI does the first draft/triage and people focus on judgement, oversight and exception handling — and the biggest individual risk is not "AI vs people" but "people with AI skills vs people without." For organisations, the response should be work redesign and reskilling, not automation for its own sake.

## Risks of adopting AI (and mitigations)

**Data risks:** poor data quality ("garbage in, garbage out"); privacy/security exposure of PII or client data, risking GDPR/compliance breach. **Model risks:** bias/fairness issues learned from historical data; lack of explainability in complex "black box" models, making decisions hard to justify to clients, auditors or regulators. **Operational risks:** model drift degrading accuracy if unmonitored; unreliable or inconsistent output, especially at latency-sensitive real-time scale. **Business risks:** wrong predictions affecting SLAs/decisions; over-dependence on AI output without validation, eroding human oversight and accountability. **Security risks:** adversarial attacks manipulating inputs to fool a model; data poisoning corrupting training data. **Compliance/governance risks:** regulatory non-compliance, missing audit trail, no formal model approval process. **Skills/adoption risks:** skills gaps in data science/AI engineering/governance; resistance to change from staff who don't trust AI outputs. **Financial risks:** high implementation cost, unclear ROI without a well-defined use case, ongoing maintenance/retraining cost. **Ethical/societal risks:** job displacement concerns, misuse through automation without control, loss of human accountability.

**Mitigation, worth stating unprompted:** data governance (quality checks, access control); model validation and explainability tooling (e.g. SHAP, LIME); continuous monitoring (drift, performance KPIs); human-in-the-loop decision-making; security controls (data protection, adversarial testing); a formal AI governance framework (approval, audit, compliance); and training/change management to support adoption. This is exactly the shape of the governance built into Hubble (see S12/B3): human-in-the-loop, DPIA, explainable model choice, and per-class performance monitoring.
