A data-driven business transformation needs a **data strategy**. You can write this after the data maturity assessment has been completed.

The data strategy is a comprehensive, long-term plan that outlines how an organisation will collect, manage, govern, utilise, and derive value from its data to support business goals.

- **Communication**:   
    It becomes the basis for convincing stakeholders of the value of the transformation.
    
- **Vision alignment:** Ensures everyone is working to the same vision.
    
- **Guidance**:   
    Although it will be a high-level document, it shows the direction of travel and helps staff to make appropriate decisions and lower-level plans that align with the overall strategy.
    
- **Progress checking:**  
    Keeps units on track towards reaching the desired end state and once the end state is reached provides direct feedback on operation, culture, and behaviour.

Key sections of the data strategy document are:

- Objectives.
    
- Data maturity assessment.
    
- Data architecture and technology.
    
- Data analytics roles and organisation.
    
- Data governance.
    
- Prioritised use cases.
    
- Data strategy roadmap.
    
- Data-driven culture.


## Objectives

In this section you will state the high-level objectives. The objectives will focus on improving data quality, accessibility, and usability to support informed decision-making and achieve business goals.

This includes ensuring data is **accurate**, **consistent**, and readily **available** for analysis, as well as fostering a data-driven culture within the organisation. An overall aim of making the organisation data-driven to obtain more reliable actionable business insights may also be given. Below are some examples of objectives:

- Improve data accuracy, consistency, and completeness across all systems and sources.
    
- Make data readily accessible and understandable for relevant stakeholders.
    
- Enable informed decision-making by providing data insights and analytics.
    
- Establish clear data governance policies and procedures to ensure responsible and ethical data usage.
    
- Foster a data-driven culture where data literacy and data analysis are valued and encouraged.
    
- Ensure that the necessary technology infrastructure and tools are in place to support data strategy implementation.
    
- Reduce data related costs such as storage, processing, and analysis.
    

The paper from [Truly Intelligent Business, no date](https://trulyintelligent.business/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/Example-Data-Strategy-Redacted.pdf) provides another example of data strategy objectives, these set at a slightly higher level:

“This data strategy identifies objectives in four key aspects: compliance, governance, value, and operations:

- **Compliance**: Ensure that we hold and use data safely.
    
- **Governance**: Assure the quality and availability of data.
    
- **Value**: Generate business benefits from data.
    
- **Operations**: Manage data efficiently and cost-effectively.”

## Sustainable data strategy

### Internal factors

Internal factors include human resources, organisational structure, technology, and corporate culture. These have been addressed in the strategy but if some business units raise initial objections, progress can be hindered. This is why corporate buy-in is so important.

### External factors

External factors include political, economic, social, technological, environmental, and legal factors. Competition and global factors such as trade policy are also external factors that can affect business operation and hence the ability to follow the strategy.

External factors are outside the control of the organisation but must be taken into consideration as potential risk factors. This is another reason regular review is important so that resilience and counter measures can be designed to protect the strategy.

### Annual review

The outcome of the annual review should be communicated to staff. In the early stages of transformation, reviews are likely to be more frequent. When the organisation has reached the dynamic end state, reviews may be less frequent.

- Purpose.
    
- Scope.
    
- Definitions.
    
- Roles and responsibilities.
	- A **data steward** liaises between data owners, custodians, and the data governance executive to define appropriate policies, arrange suitable technical support, and ensure policy is being followed.
	    
	- A **data custodian** is responsible for the technical management and security of data, ensuring secure storage and access according to predefined rules.
	    
	- A **data owner** ensures the quality, accuracy, and integrity of the data within a domain, including defining and enforcing data policies, managing data access, and ensuring compliance with relevant regulations and standards.

	- This section outlines data organisation protocols, including standards for classification, labelling, and storage. It should also define procedures for data access, usage, and retention, ensuring data is consistently managed and accessible.
