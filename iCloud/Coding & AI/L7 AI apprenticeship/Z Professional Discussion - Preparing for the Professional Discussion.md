## \***\*K19\*\***: The principles and properties behind statistical and machine learning methods

  

Principles behind **Statistics**: quantitative analysis, deductive, gaining knowledge from data

  

**Descriptive statistics**: working with collections of data to abstract useful information about the collection (mean, standard deviation, range, distribution, shape(skewness, kurtosis)

  

**Inferential statistics**: hypothesising whether characteristics of a sample of data can describe the population as a whole. Statistical significance testing is used to test hypotheses. Statistical significance level is a probability test based on characteristics of normal distribution giving a result a confidence level regarding whether results are significant or not.

  

**Machine learning**: the machine learns characteristics of data by training on large data sets. Iteration is used until the machine settles on an answer. Learning can be supervised, unsupervised, reinforcement or semi-supervised. For supervised learning one field will be the label (or target) and the algorithm will attempt to produce an equation such that given new data it can predict the value of the label field. In unsupervised learning, the machine compares data characteristics and proposes clusters based on similarity.

  

**Reinforcement learning:** the machine learns through trial and error gaining rewards for good decisions and punishments for poor decisions. In Reinforcement Learning (RL), the problem to resolve is represented as a network of states and actions with the machine deciding what action to take at any given state. This is called a Markov Decision Process (MDP). The MDP is an extension of the Markov chain with the concept of decisions and rewards added Theoretical results in Reinforcement Learning rely on the MDP description being a correct match to the problem. RL uses an underlying model - the Markov chain

  

**Semi-supervised learning**: Semi-supervised learning utilizes both labelled and unlabelled data; in this way, as the name suggests, it is a hybrid technique between supervised and unsupervised learning. In general, the core idea of semi-supervision is to treat a datapoint differently based on whether it has a label or not: for labelled points, the algorithm will use traditional supervision to update the model weights; and for unlabelled points, the algorithm minimizes the difference in predictions between other similar training examples.

  

Make sure you know the differences between the main types of machine learning algorithm ( supervised, unsupervised, (semi-supervised), reinforcement learning). [Explained: Neural networks | MIT News | Massachusetts Institute of Technology](https://news.mit.edu/2017/explained-neural-networks-deep-learning-0414)

  

A **neural network** is made up of connected nodes meant to imitate neurons in the human brain. There is an input layer, an output layer and a number of hidden layers. Various values and weights are set for the hidden layer nodes. At the receiving node, each input value is multiplied by its weight and then the results are totalled up. If the result is higher than a certain threshold the node is activated and sends its results to the next layer. There are various activation functions. Common ones are Sigmoid (transforms results to a value between 0 and 1), Tanh (outputs a value between -1 and +1) and Relu (outputs any result greater than 0). Once a prediction is made it is checked against the true answer and the loss is calculated. Weights are then adjusted to reduce the loss with an eventual aim of minimising it. This process is known as gradient descent (see below under K22 and "Calculus").

**Probability**

  

**_Standard Statistics_**

  

As far as **standard statistics** goes, we saw the use of probability in statistical significance testing in Data Science Principles. The normal distribution (also known as a Gaussian distribution or probability bell curve), on which many statistical significance tests are based, is a probabilistic distribution which tells us the likelihood or probability of value being a particular number of standard deviations from the mean. Thus, we can use critical values to assess whether our results are significant or not according to various confidence levels. Typically, if a result is different to the mean by around two standard deviations it is considered to be significant.

  

It is recognised that there is always uncertainty in data. This can be due to human error, instrument error, sampling, or just naturally occurring phenomena. Probability theory can help us handle uncertainty. Given an assumption of a probability distribution (e.g. a normal distribution) we can use confidence levels to gauge likelihood of correctness. Other distributions can also be used e.g. poisson distribution. The **confidence level** is the percentage of times you expect to get close to the same estimate if you run your experiment again or resample the population in the same way. The [**confidence interval**](https://www.scribbr.co.uk/stats/confidence-interval-meaning/) consists of the upper and lower bounds of the estimate you expect to find at a given level of confidence. For example, if you are estimating a 95% confidence interval around the mean proportion of female babies born every year based on a random sample of babies, you might find an upper bound of 0.56 and a lower bound of 0.48. These are the upper and lower bounds of the confidence interval. The confidence level is 95%.

**_Decision Trees_**

  

Decision trees might also be designed to output probability of an item belonging to a class. In Random Forest this can be done by counting the number of trees that give a positive result compared to those that give a negative result and then obtaining a probability. In a single decision tree, it might be done by noting levels of support at each branch and calculating probability from that.

  

**_Softmax_**

  

**W**e see the SoftMax function used in neural networks for classification problems. Softmax is implemented through a neural network layer just before the output layer as the final activation function. Softmax assigns decimal probabilities to each class in a multi-class problem. Those decimal probabilities must add up to 1.0. It converts the linear output to probabilities. Sigmoid is used for binary classification methods where we only have two classes, while SoftMax applies to multiclass problems. In fact, the SoftMax function is an extension of the Sigmoid function. Softmax is used when we need a multinomial probability output, i. e. multi-class classification problem.

  

**_Log Loss_**

  

Log loss (aka binary cross-entropy) is frequently used as a loss function when training a binary classifier .

  

![[993bb545f9400ce32b8591dd6f0d46aa_MD5.png]]

  

Extract 6. Log Loss Formula (Source: Godoy, 2018)

  

Yi can be 0 (negative) or 1 (positive) given this is binary classification. p(yi) is the probability of yi being 1 ( as predicted by the hypothesised model). The function adds up the logs of the probability of each element belonging to a class and then takes the average, Note if yi is positive (i.e. 1) only the first term of the expression following the sigma sign gets evaluated. On the other hand if yi is negative (i.e. 0)only the second term gets evaluated.

  

**Evaluation example**

  

Let's say we have three elements: y1= 1, y2 =0, y3=1. Let's say the ML model predicted the probabilities for y<sub>1</sub>, y<sub>2</sub> and y<sub>3</sub> being positive respectively as follows:

  

p(y<sub>1</sub>) == 0.66, p(y<sub>2</sub>)=0.33, p(y<sub>3</sub>)= 0.66

  

Note Log 0.66 = -0.18

  

Evaluating expression to left of the sigma sign ( call it LoSS)

  

for y<sub>1</sub>: LoSS= 1\*log(0.66) = 1\* -0.18 = -0.18

  

for y<sub>2</sub>: LoSS= 1 \*log(1-0.33) = 1\*log(0.66) = 1\* -0.18

  

for y<sub>3</sub>: LoSS = 1\*log(0.66) = 1\* -0.18

  

Evaluate the sigma (add them all up) = -0.54

  

Divide by N = -0.18

  

Apply the minus (sign at start of expression)

  

Log loss = 0.18

How does a neural network work?

  

The input data needs to be organised to represent features. Each row represents a particular combination of features. The input layer of the neural network consists of a number of nodes. Each node will accept a feature value of a particular case ( a row of the dataset will represent a case).

  

In a classification problem the output layer will include nodes to represent each possible outcome. The output nodes would have different values with the highest node representing the predicted class. Probabilities for each class can be predicted for the output layer by using Softmax. For a regression problem the output layer will contain a node to represent the predicted value.

  

In between input and output layers will be at least one hidden layer of nodes. The number of nodes in the hidden layer (or layers) is set by the developer (data scientist). The nodes are connected by links and each link is assigned a random weight. The values received by the input layer are fed through to the nodes in the hidden layer after being multiplied by the weight on the link and then the incoming values are added up in the receiving node (linear regression). A bias weight might also be input to the node (hence the node calculates a regression expression y=mx+b). An activation function is applied to moderate the summation, and the resulting value is passed to the next layer applying the same process of weight multiplication with summation and activation then occurring in the receiving node resulting in a value to be passed to the nest layer and so on. This process is called feed forward. When the output layer is reached , the resulting value is compared to the labelled value from the case (supervised learning) and a loss is calculated. The input data is processed in batches. When the end of a batch is reached the average loss is calculated. The learning algorithm aims to minimise the loss and uses gradient descent to do this. The weights in the links connecting to the output layer are adjusted according to the loss according to a learning rate (effects the gradient descent) . The change in the weights continues to be backpropagated to each previous layer until the input layer is reached. The process continues with the next batch of input cases until the end of the input data set is is reached. This will count as one pass through the data set. Each pass through is called an epoch. There will be multiple passes through. Finally, the network will be trained, and the final weights achieved will be the parameters of the model.

You decide on a number of hyperparameter ,e,g. activation function for each layer, loss function, optimisation function, learning rate, batch size, number of epochs, early stopping. Explain Train-Validate-Test

  

The data set is split into three portions: training; validation; and test.

  

The training data is a portion of the data set used for training. The validation set is used for tuning. The test data is an unseen data used only after the model is fully trained to evaluate the model.

  

Where does cross-validation fit into Train-Validate-Test ?

  

Cross-validation combines the train-validate phase so there is no need for a separate validation data set. The training data is split into k folds and then there are k iterations of training with a different fold held out for validation each time. The procedure returns the optimised data model. That model can then be tested with unseen data.

  

What is the difference between validation and testing ?

  

Validation can be seen as the initial testing you do on the model prior to and during the optimisation stage. Testing is the final unseen data that you use to fairly test and eventually evaluate the usefulness of the model.

  

What is hyperparameter tuning?

  

Hyperparameters are parameters whose values control the learning process and determine the final model parameters . Examples are:

  

- Train-test split ratio

- Learning rate in optimization algorithms (e.g. gradient descent)

- Choice of optimization algorithm (e.g., gradient descent, stochastic gradient descent, or Adam optimizer)

- Choice of activation function in a neural network (nn) layer (e.g. Sigmoid, ReLU, Tanh)

- The choice of cost or loss function the model will use

- Number of hidden layers in a neural network

- Number of activation units in each layer

- The drop-out rate in nn (dropout probability)

- Number of iterations (epochs) in training a nn

- Number of clusters in a clustering task

- Kernel or filter size in convolutional layers

- Pooling size

- Batch size

  

Hyperparameter tuning means testing various combinations of hyperparameter values to see which gives the best result.

  

How can hyperparameters be adjusted without overfitting?

  

- Use more training data.

- Early stopping - stop training after a certain number of iterations - this stops the algorithm learning the noise in the data.

- After testing hyperparameter combinations, do not pick very highest scoring combination

- Drop nodes in a neural network

- Pruning - identify the most important features in the data set and eliminate irrelevant ones

- Limit number of branches in a decision tree

  

Regularisation

  

Regularisation is a set of training/ optimisation techniques that seek to avoid overfitting. The techniques assess the impact of features on prediction and apply penalties. Small penalties are applied to weight/feature combinations which have greater impact and large penalties are applied to weigh/feature combinations with small impact.

  

There are three main regularization techniques, namely:

  

- Ridge Regression (L2 Norm)

- Lasso (L1 Norm)

- Dropout.

WCAG 2.2 is the latest international standard from the [World Wide Web Consortium (W3C)](https://www.w3.org/) (W3C) for making web content, websites, and apps accessible to people with various disabilities, including those with vision, hearing, mobility, and cognitive/learning impairments. It builds on previous versions by adding new criteria, particularly for cognitive, learning, and mobility disabilities, and for users with low vision and on mobile devices. Conforming to WCAG 2.2, especially at Level AA, is a legal requirement for many public sector websites and apps, such as those in the UK government.

  

Key Aspects of WCAG 2.2

  

- **Purpose:**

  

To provide a set of recommendations for making digital content accessible to a wider range of users, including those with cognitive differences like autism, dyslexia, and ADHD.

  

- **Scope:**

  

It applies to websites, mobile apps, and other digital services.

  

- **Conformance Levels:**

  

WCAG has three levels of conformance: A (lowest), AA (intermediate), and AAA (highest). Level AA is a common standard for public sector bodies.

  

- **New Success Criteria:**

  

WCAG 2.2 introduces new success criteria (9 in total) to the existing WCAG 2.1 guidelines, focusing on areas such as:

  

- - **Cognitive & Learning Disabilities:** Addressing needs related to ADHD, Autism, Dyslexia, and other learning difficulties.

    - **Mobile & Low Vision Users:** Enhancing accessibility for people using touchscreens and those with low vision.

    - **Input Methods:** Including criteria for keyboard, mouse, and touchscreen input methods and gestures.

- **Mandatory for Public Sector:**

  

In the UK, public sector organizations are legally required to meet WCAG 2.2 Level AA standards for patient and staff-facing services.

  

Why is WCAG 2.2 Important?

  

- **Legal & Policy Compliance:**

  

Adopting WCAG 2.2 helps organizations meet legal accessibility requirements and aligns with future policy changes.

  

- **Wider Audience:**

  

It allows more people with disabilities to access and use digital services.

  

- **Improved User Experience:**

  

By addressing diverse user needs, WCAG 2.2 contributes to a more inclusive and user-friendly digital environment for everyone..

  

**Diversity of user needs - diverse training sets**

Diverse data sets improve a model's ability to generalize. This leads to accurate predictions on new, unknown data.

Transferring data between countries?

  

UK "adequacy regulations" set out in law that the legal framework in that country, territory, or international organisation, or in a particular sector in a country or territory, has been assessed as providing 'adequate' protection for people's rights and freedoms about their personal data.

  

The UK has adequacy regulations about the following countries and territories:

  

- The European Economic Area (EEA) countries; These are the EU member states and the EFTA States (Iceland, Norway and Liechtenstein).  

   <br/>Also covered by "adequacy regulations" are:

- EU or EEA institutions, bodies, offices or agencies

- Gibraltar

- The Republic of Korea; and

- Countries, territories and sectors covered by the European Commission's adequacy decisions

  

In addition, the partial findings of adequacy about:

  

- Canada - only covers data that is subject to Canada's Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA). Not all data is subject to PIPEDA. Please read the [guidance on the scope of PIPEDA](https://www.priv.gc.ca/en/privacy-topics/privacy-laws-in-canada/the-personal-information-protection-and-electronic-documents-act-pipeda/pipeda_brief/) from the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada for further information

- Japan - only covers personal data transferred to private sector organisations subject to Japan's Act on the Protection of Personal Information. This does not include transfers of the types listed in the [EU's adequacy decision for Japan](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32019D0419&qid=1694166958556#d1e2449-1-1).

- The United States of America - only covers data which is transferred under the UK Extension to the EU-US Data Privacy Framework. You can find more information about the UK Extension, including [a factsheet for UK organisations](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/uk-us-data-bridge-supporting-documents/uk-us-data-bridge-factsheet-for-uk-organisations), on [gov.uk](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/uk-us-data-bridge-supporting-documents) and on the US Department of Commerce's [Data Privacy Framework Program website](https://www.dataprivacyframework.gov/s/).

  

UK companies may make a restricted transfer if the receiver is located in a third country or territory, or is an international organisation, or in a particular sector in a country or territory, covered by UK 'adequacy regulations'.

  

If not covered by adequacy regulation, the GDPR does allow a transfer if the controller or processor has provided "appropriate safeguards".

  

Appropriate safeguards include:

  

- Standard data protection clauses (SCCs)

- Binding corporate rules "BCRs"

- Approved Codes of Conduct

- Approved certification mechanisms

- A legally binding and enforceable instrument between public authorities or bodies

  

What ethical concerns are there in AI ?

  

Areas of ethical concern in AI are:

  

Transparency

  

Accountability

  

Bias

  

Accuracy

  

Privacy

  

GDPR goes some way to allay concerns particularly regarding privacy. It also has some rules regarding automated decision making (see Article 22 at [Rights related to automated decision making including profiling | ICO](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/individual-rights/individual-rights/rights-related-to-automated-decision-making-including-profiling/) )

  

Given the growth in AI and concerns around ethical issues a number of frameworks have been introduced to provide guidance.

  

Asilomar Principles ([Asilomar AI principles announced - Future of Humanity Institute (ox.ac.uk)](https://www.fhi.ox.ac.uk/asilomar-ai-principles-announced/)

  

OECD AI guidelines a at [OECD Legal Instruments](https://legalinstruments.oecd.org/en/instruments/OECD-LEGAL-0449)

  

OECD UK Government guidance at [Understanding artificial intelligence ethics and safety - GOV.UK (www.gov.uk)](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/understanding-artificial-intelligence-ethics-and-safety)

  

European AI ACT. The European AI Act is a regulation on artificial intelligence (AI) that will be in effect in the European Union from August 2, 2026. It is the first comprehensive regulation on AI from a major regulator. [EU AI Act: first regulation on artificial intelligence | Topics | European Parliament](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/topics/en/article/20230601STO93804/eu-ai-act-first-regulation-on-artificial-intelligence)

  

Also note UK's framework for AI Regulation. ( [The UK's framework for AI regulation | Deloitte UK](https://www.deloitte.com/uk/en/Industries/financial-services/blogs/the-uks-framework-for-ai-regulation.html) ) The UK's principles for responsible AI are:

  

- 1. Safety, security and robustness

  - Appropriate transparency and explainability

  - Fairness

  - Accountability and governance

  - Contestability and redress
