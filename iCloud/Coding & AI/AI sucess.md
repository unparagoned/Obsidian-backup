# Maths

>The solution came following a very simple chatbot inquiry. OpenAI mathematicians fed the conjecture into a large language model (LLM) and asked it if Erdős was wrong. The researchers were not involved in the initial math calculations but stepped in afterward to review the software's workings and help rewrite the findings.
>Instead of trying to arrange dots on a flat piece of paper right away, the AI program used different branches of mathematics that involved geometry and complex number systems. It produced hundreds of pages of calculations and eventually concluded that there was no universal speed limit as Erdős had proposed. Instead, the software proved that you can pack dots together in a way that creates vastly more equal distances than anyone previously thought possible.
>But, of course, we can't just take AI's words for it. The mathematical proof was checked and verified by experts who confirmed the core discovery presented in the paper: "We disprove the unit distance conjecture; our main theorem is as follows: There exists an absolute constant δ > 0 and infinitely many positive integers n for which ν(n) ≥ n1+δ."
>The breakthrough is notable not only because it cracks a long-standing puzzle posed by one of the 20th century's greatest mathematicians, but also because of the software's pivotal role. This marks one of the clearest examples so far of AI helping to solve a longstanding mathematical problem that had stumped the experts.
