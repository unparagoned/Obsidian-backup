# How much sleep do you need

The actual amount of sleep you need depends on what you did that day. If you spent all day doing vigorous training or a lot of manual labour, you might need ten hours of sleep, on the other hand, if you just spent it in front of Netflix you might only need eight hours of sleep. Which is why I recommend you just wake up naturally once you've had enough sleep.

Your probably shouldn't be using an alarm to wake yourself up, alarms by definition will wake you up when your body still thinks you needs more sleep plus it's usually in the middle of sleep cycle which can make you feel particularly groggy. Plus alarm clocks are quite stressful for the body. Sure use an alarm clock to tell you when to get out of bed or as a backup, but you should be going to sleep early enough that you wake up naturally once you've had enough sleep. Also using the snooze button just leads to more stress on the body and poor quality sleep.



There is a U shape mortality curve when it comes to how much you sleep, and I would say that half of that is causal and the other half is reverse causation. So if you are getting less than 7-8h of sleep then that has a negative effect on your mortality. If you are getting lots of sleep then that probably means you are ill or have some kind of health issue and your body needs as much sleep as possible to fight the illness. If say your have easy stages of cancer or something and are getting 10h of sleep that will contribute to higher mortality rates. But if you told those people sleeping 10h to sleep only 7-8h of sleep where overall mortality is minimised they are going to have even higher mortality rates. Also people with say sleep apnea which interrupts their sleep, have bodies which is making them sleep longer to try and makeup for the poor quality sleep. If you told these people to get less actual sleep that would be bad for them

>In general, sleep duration is associated with mortality in a U-shaped fashion, such that the lowest risk is most often found in the group who report sleep durations of 7‚Äì8 hours
[https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2856739/](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2856739/)

## Regularity

Your body is supposed to work on a fairly regular schedule. Your circadian rhythm should be strong and it sets things in motion for when they should happen. 

So your body releases hormones and stuff before and when you should be alseep according to your circadian rhythm, not just when you go to sleep. So your body will be primed and release hormones for your normal bedtime, and if you go to sleep later you won't get all the benefits even if you sleep for as long.

## Temperature

Temperature is a major factor in your circadian rhythm, after you wake up your temperature will increase and peak during the day, then decrease when it comes time to sleep.

Having a hot bath or shower in the evening, results in vasodilation which let's your body then cool down faster. So paradoxically a hot bath in the evening allows your body to to cool down which helps with sleep.

General cold water baths can help with sleep, not this isn't related to just before you sleep but in general. Some people have them before they sleep but others find them too stimulating.

### Cognitive Shuffle

The approach moves you from of a high state of alertness, towards drifting off to sleep. Cognitive shuffling scrambles your thoughts so that your brain can't try to make sense of things. It interrupts the processes of memory, evaluation, planning, scheduling and problem solving.

Here's how it's done.  
1. Pick a word such as "bedtime".
2. Begin with the first letter "b" and think of a word that starts with the letter "b". For example "broom". Bring up the mental image of a broom. Then think of another word that begins with the letter "b" and visualise that word. Keep going until you run out of words that begin with the letter "b".
3. Go to the next letter in "bedtime" - "e", and think of words that begin with "e". Visualise the words as you think of them.
4. Continue at your own pace, but just keep going until you fall asleep.¬†Dr Beaudoin recommends if stressful thoughts come your way, just¬†acknowledge them and go back to¬†shuffling your thoughts instead.
5. An additional tip: Breathe in as you come up with the word. Breathe out as you visualise the word. If it takes longer to visualise the word, you'll be giving yourself lovely long out breaths which will calm your body.
