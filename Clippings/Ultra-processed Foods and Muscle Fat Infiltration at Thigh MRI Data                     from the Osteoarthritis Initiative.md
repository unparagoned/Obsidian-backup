## Abstract

### Background

The relationship between high ultra-processed food (UPF) consumption and musculoskeletal health remains underexplored.

### Purpose

To assess the relationship between UPF consumption and thigh muscle fat infiltration (MFI) in a population at risk for knee osteoarthritis (KOA), using thigh MRI.

### Materials and Methods

This cross-sectional secondary analysis uses baseline data from the Osteoarthritis Initiative (February 2004 to October 2015). Participants were at risk for KOA but free of radiographic osteoarthritis (Kellgren-Lawrence grade ≤ 1) and pain in either knee or hip. MFI was assessed using Goutallier grade (grade 0 \[no fatty streaks\] to grade 4 \[>50% fatty signal intensity\]) on axial T1-weighted spin-echo MRI scans. Standardized sum Goutallier grades were calculated for bilateral flexors, extensors, and adductors and all thigh muscles. Standardized proportion of UPF in daily diet was calculated using the NOVA diet classification and a self-reported food frequency questionnaire for the preceding 12 months. The relationship between standardized UPF consumption and Goutallier grades was tested using linear regression models adjusted for sociodemographic characteristics, total daily calories, smoking, physical activity, depression, and either body mass index (BMI, calculated as weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared) or abdominal circumference (AC). Interaction between UPF consumption and sex was also assessed.

### Results

A total of 615 participants were included (mean age, 59.5 years ± 9 \[SD\]; 340 women; mean BMI, 27 ± 4.3; mean AC, 98.9 cm ± 12.1); UPFs constituted a mean 41.4% ± 13.4 of their diet. In BMI-adjusted models, greater UPF consumption was associated with higher MFI in all thigh muscles (β = 0.108 \[95% CI: 0.029, 0.186\]; *P* =.007), bilateral flexors (β = 0.111 \[95% CI: 0.030, 0.192\]; *P* =.008), and bilateral adductors (β = 0.122 \[95% CI: 0.040, 0.204\]; *P* =.004). Stronger relationships were observed for all muscle groups in AC-adjusted models (all muscles: β = 0.134 \[95% CI: 0.052, 0.215\], *P* =.001; flexors: β = 0.133 \[95% CI: 0.050, 0.217\], *P* =.002; extensors: β = 0.097 \[95% CI: 0.015, 0.178\], *P* =.02; adductors: β = 0.147 \[95% CI: 0.061, 0.232\], *P* =.001). Sex interactions were not statistically significant (all *P* >.05).

### Conclusion

Regardless of sex, high UPF consumption in individuals at risk for KOA was associated with higher muscle fat content on thigh MRI scans.

## Key Results

- ■ In this cross-sectional secondary analysis using baseline data from 615 participants from the Osteoarthritis Initiative, Goutallier grading on T1-weighted thigh MRI scans showed that thigh muscle fat infiltration (MFI) was related to diet quality, regardless of sex (*P* >.05 for interactions).
- ■ In individuals at risk for knee osteoarthritis, greater ultra-processed food (UPF) consumption was associated with higher Goutallier grades in all thigh muscles (β = 0.108; *P* =.007), flexors (β = 0.111; *P* =.008), and adductors (β = 0.122; *P* =.004) in body mass index (BMI)–adjusted models.
- ■ Abdominal circumference–adjusted models were superior to BMI-adjusted models in characterizing relationships between UPF consumption and MFI (all muscles: β = 0.134, *P* =.001; flexors: β = 0.133, *P* =.002; extensors: β = 0.097, *P* =.02; adductors: β = 0.147, *P* =.001).

## Introduction

The global rise in obesity is related to the increased proportion of ultra-processed food (UPF) in modern diets ([^1]). In the United States, at least one in four adults has obesity ([^2]), with UPFs contributing on average 58% of dietary energy intake and 89% of added sugar ([^1]). UPFs include foods and beverages that are made with industrial-scale processing methods and various chemical additives to enhance shelf life and appeal ([^3]). A diet high in UPFs is of low quality, leading to inadequate intake of essential nutrients such as fiber, protein, and micronutrients. The high levels of salt, sugar, and fat in UPFs replace natural food sources, contributing to obesity and various health issues, including abdominal obesity, metabolic syndrome, cardiometabolic disease, frailty, depression, cancer, and increased all-cause mortality ([^4] – [^6]).

Osteoarthritis is a whole-organ disorder involving multiple tissues, including intra- or periarticular fat and muscles ([^7],[^8]). Obesity is a well-recognized risk factor for osteoarthritis of the knee, the most commonly affected joint, and knee osteoarthritis (KOA) is among the most disabling and costly diseases worldwide ([^9]). Thigh muscle functionality is crucial for knee stability. Muscle functional decline can both contribute to and result from reduced activity and decreased mobility, playing a dual role in the comorbidities associated with KOA ([^8]). Changes in thigh muscle composition, specifically increased intramuscular fat in the knee extensors, may result from disuse atrophy due to knee pain or from generalized muscle impairment and systemic inflammation in an aging population with high body mass index (BMI) ([^10]).

Recent findings indicate that greater UPF consumption is associated with greater knee pain, thinner knee cartilage, slower gait speed, and fewer chair stands in timed assessment, warranting further investigation into the relationship between UPF consumption and skeletal muscle quality ([^11]). Additionally, large studies indicate that greater UPF consumption is associated with lower skeletal muscle mass and higher body fat percentage, after accounting for sociodemographic characteristics and comorbidities ([^12],[^13]). Ultimately, obesity links unhealthy dietary habits and KOA, compromising the functional, qualitative, and quantitative features of thigh muscles. However, the relationship between poor diet quality, marked by high intake of UPFs, and skeletal muscle quality remains underexplored. Investigating this relationship in an early osteoarthritis or osteoarthritis-free but at-risk population is essential before exploring complex interactions in patients with established disease.

Thus, the aim of this study was to assess the relationship between UPF consumption and thigh muscle fat infiltration (MFI), measured using thigh MRI, in a population at risk for KOA.

## Materials and Methods

### Study Participants

This cross-sectional secondary analysis used baseline data from the Osteoarthritis Initiative (OAI), which was funded as a public-private partnership by the National Institutes of Health and industry (Pfizer, Merck, GlaxoSmithKline, and Novartis) *([https://www.niams.nih.gov/grants-funding/funded-research/osteoarthritis-initiative](https://www.niams.nih.gov/grants-funding/funded-research/osteoarthritis-initiative))*. The OAI was a multicenter, longitudinal prospective study from February 2004 to October 2015 focusing on the evaluation of biomarkers for KOA ([ClinicalTrials.gov](https://clinicaltrials.gov/) ID: NCT00080171) ([^14]). The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act–compliant study protocol, amendments, and informed consent documentation were approved by the institutional review boards of all participating centers ([^15]).

To minimize the confounding effects of disuse muscle atrophy due to pain and disability related to knee or hip osteoarthritis or systemic comorbidities, this study included a subset of participants from the OAI who did not have radiographic disease or pain in the knee or hip joints or self-reported systemic comorbidities at the baseline OAI visit. Participants were excluded if they had radiographic KOA (Kellgren-Lawrence \[KL\] grade > 1) or if, on weight-bearing pelvis radiographs, at least one hip was graded as “definite radiographic osteoarthritis” according to the Osteoarthritis Research Society International atlas ([^16] – [^18]). Participants with knee or hip pain, aching, or stiffness on most days of the month for the right or left knee or hip; participants with rheumatoid arthritis or other inflammatory arthritis; and participants lacking thigh MRI or plausible dietary data on the food frequency questionnaire (FFQ) were also excluded. Furthermore, patients with systemic diseases (stroke, diabetes, or cancer) were excluded, as detailed in [Figure 1](#fig1). Participants in this study overlapped with those in a previous study investigating the relationships between thigh MFI and demographic features ([^19]).

### Assessment of Diet Quality

Participants completed self-reported nutritional surveys, including the Block Brief 2000 FFQ ([^20]), assessing their dietary habits over the preceding 12 months. A total of 102 questions about various foods, beverages, culinary ingredients, and oral supplemental vitamins and minerals were available for review from the baseline OAI visit. The frequency of consumption for each food item was reported on an eight-tier scale, ranging from “never” to “every day.” The quantity of food consumed was documented using standard units or portion sizes ([^20]).

The annual consumption of food and beverages was calculated in “servings per year” by multiplying the reported frequency by the quantity consumed. Then, the NOVA diet classification system was applied, and each question was assessed to determine whether it belonged to the NOVA-4 group (also referred to as UPFs) or one of the other NOVA groups (groups 1–3), which represent unprocessed or minimally processed foods, culinary ingredients, and processed foods ([^3]). Since its introduction in 2010, the NOVA diet classification has become the most widely used international research tool for UPFs ([^21]). In this diet classification system, the focus is on the nature, extent, and purposes of processing during preparation rather than the total nutrients derived from each food item ([^3]). The NOVA-4 dietary items we aimed to isolate from the FFQ are, by definition, ready-to-eat foods and beverages prepared using industrial-level chemical processing methods. These items are high in cosmetic additives such as flavorings, colorings, and emulsifiers, designed to enhance shelf life, palatability, and consumer appeal. NOVA-4 items are extensively documented via lists of specific examples in the literature, and these lists were referenced to identify UPFs based on the FFQ ([^3]). Once UPFs were identified, the proportion of UPF in the overall daily diet (as a percentage), based on the annual consumption of food and beverages from the preceding 12 months, was calculated for each participant. Questions about supplemental vitamin and mineral intakes were excluded from these calculations. Dietary data on total daily calories (kilocalories per day) and dietary fat content, calculated using United States Department of Agriculture food composition data evaluated by NutritionQuest *([www.nutritionquest.com](http://www.nutritionquest.com/))* ([^20]), were available in the OAI database.

### Thigh MRI Protocol

Thigh MRI scans were obtained using a 3.0-T Magnetom Trio scanner system (Siemens Healthineers) at all centers. MFI was assessed using semiquantitative five-level Goutallier grading (ranging from 0 \[no fatty streaks in the muscle\] to 4 \[>50% fatty signal intensity\]) on axial T1-weighted spin-echo scans (repetition time msec/echo time msec, 500/10; spatial resolution, 0.977 × 0.977 mm; field of view, 500 mm; section thickness, 5 mm; section gap, 0 mm) ([^22]). A slab of 15 contiguous axial image sections, covering the middle third of the femur, was acquired, starting 10 cm proximal to the distal epiphysis and extending 7.5 cm proximally. Further details about image acquisition are available in the OAI MRI protocol ([^15]). Owing to the fixed distance (10 cm) between the distal femoral epiphysis and the most distal image acquired, the thigh muscles included in the field of view varied depending on each participant’s femoral length ([^23]). Thus, rather than relying on a single axial image, all 15 axial images were used to assess MFI.

### Assessment of Thigh MFI on MRI Scans

Using IrfanView (version 4.62; *[https://www.irfanview.com/](https://www.irfanview.com/)*) and blinded to the demographic, clinical, and dietary data of the participants, two independent trained observers (Z.A. and W.M.S., with >10 years of experience and in training, respectively) scored a total of 10 muscles per thigh (rectus femoris, vastus medialis, vastus lateralis, vastus intermedius, biceps femoris, semimembranosus, semitendinosus, gracilis, sartorius, and adductors) on both sides. A Goutallier grade was assigned to a muscle if the muscle was consistently observed in at least eight of the 15 consecutive axial images, irrespective of localized areas of different grades if they appeared focally or on only a few image sections. Then sum scores for functional groups of knee extensors (rectus femoris, vastus medialis, vastus lateralis, and vastus intermedius), knee flexors (semimembranosus, semitendinosus, and biceps femoris), and thigh adductors (adductors, gracilis, and sartorius) and sum scores for bilateral thigh muscles from all groups were calculated per participant.

### Statistical Analysis

Statistical analyses were performed by two authors (Z.A. and G.B.J.) using Stata version 18 (StataCorp). To maximize generalizability and to avoid selection bias, data from all eligible participants were used, and no a priori power analysis was performed for this observational secondary analysis. Baseline participant characteristics were analyzed using descriptive statistics. Continuous variables are presented as means ± SDs, and categorical variables are summarized as counts and percentages.

To enable comparison across different models, both the UPF predictor variable (proportion of UPF in the daily diet) and the sum Goutallier grades were standardized by adjusting their means to 0 and their SDs to 1. Linear regression models were used to assess the relationship between standardized UPF and standardized Goutallier grades. Results from all models are standardized β coefficients indicating the SD change in the sum Goutallier grade for a 1-SD increase in the UPF predictor.

The linearity of the relationship between the predictor (standardized UPF) and the outcome variables (standardized sum Goutallier grades) was assessed by including a quadratic term in standardized UPF. When the quadratic term was not statistically significant, the results were consistent with linear associations.

Covariates included age, sex, self-reported race (Asian, Black, Other, and White) and ethnicity (Hispanic and non-Hispanic) assessed as part of the OAI design and available in the OAI database, total daily caloric intake (kilocalories per day), Physical Activity Scale for the Elderly score ([^24]), cumulative smoking (pack-years), presence of depression (a score of ≥16 on the Center for Epidemiological Studies Depression Scale) ([^25]), education, income level, and either BMI (calculated as weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared) (model 1) or abdominal circumference (AC) (model 2) ([^26]). AC is a measure of central obesity that captures fat distribution and serves as an indicator of cardiometabolic health ([^27]). To evaluate potential differences in the relationship between UPF consumption and Goutallier grades by sex, an interaction between UPF consumption and sex was included in the models. Statistical significance was defined as *P* <.05.

Sensitivity analyses were performed to explore the relationships between UPF consumption and Goutallier grades with additional adjustments for total dietary fat (in grams) and for knees without radiographic KOA (KL grade 0) versus with doubtful radiographic KOA (KL grade 1). Because the leg-level analysis included multiple knees for some participants, mixed effects models with random intercepts per participant were used to accommodate potential correlation within participants.

To assess the reproducibility of the Goutallier grading, the two trained observers rescored Goutallier grade for 100 randomly selected cases (200 thighs) more than 6 weeks after the initial grading. Reproducibility was measured using weighted Cohen κ, where 0 indicates no agreement and 1 indicates perfect agreement, and cluster resampled bootstrapping with 1000 repetitions was used to derive 95% CIs to account for clustered observations (two thighs per person).

Of the 615 participants, 397 (64.6%) were overweight (BMI ≥ 25 but <30) and 149 (24.2%) were obese (BMI ≥ 30). Mean AC was similar for men and women: 98 cm ± 12.4 and 100.1 cm ± 11.7, respectively. According to World Health Organization definitions for abdominal obesity (a waist circumference of ≥102 cm in men and ≥88 cm in women) ([^27]), 42.5% (117 of 275) of the men and 80.6% (270 of 335) of the women qualified as having abdominal obesity.

Most of the participants (431 of 600 \[71.8%\] participants with available income data) self-reported an annual income greater than $50 000, which qualified them as having income in the top half of the income scale at the OAI baseline visit. More than half of the participants (338 of 588 \[57.5%\] participants with available smoking data) did not have a history of smoking.

The mean proportion of UPF in the overall annual diet was 41.4% ± 13.4. The mean daily caloric intake was 1381 kcal/d ± 518, with men consuming more daily calories than women (mean, 1566 kcal/d ± 563 vs 1231 kcal/d ± 423). UPF consumption was also greater in men than in women (mean, 44.9% ± 13.3 vs 38.6% ± 12.8). [Table 1](#tbl1) summarizes the characteristics of the study sample.

### Thigh Muscle Characteristics at MRI

The flexor and extensor muscles exhibited the highest and lowest sum Goutallier grades, indicating the greatest and lowest MFI, respectively (mean sum Goutallier grade: flexors, 8.93 ± 3.30; extensors, 7.66 ± 4.38; adductors, 8.56 ± 3.28; all thigh muscles, 25.15 ± 10.06). Women had greater MFI than men across all muscle groups (mean sum Goutallier grade: all thigh muscles, 26.31 ± 9.64 vs 23.70 ± 10.39; extensors, 8.21 ± 4.20 vs 6.97 ± 4.50; flexors, 9.27 ± 3.13 vs 8.51 ± 3.46; adductors, 8.84 ± 3.18 vs 8.22 ± 3.38).

Results for inter- and intraobserver reproducibility of thigh muscle Goutallier grading indicated a high degree of agreement, with κ ranging from 0.67 (bootstrapped 95% CI: 0.54, 0.80) for the rectus femoris to 0.86 (bootstrapped 95% CI: 0.78, 0.95) for the biceps femoris for interobserver agreement, and from 0.85 (bootstrapped 95% CI: 0.79, 0.91) for the biceps femoris to 0.92 (bootstrapped 95% CI: 0.88, 0.97) for the semitendinosus for intraobserver agreement. Detailed results of the reproducibility analyses were reported previously ([^19]).

### Associations between UPF Consumption and MFI at MRI

The linearity analysis demonstrated statistically nonsignificant *P* values for the interaction term in each muscle group (*P* value for interaction for all muscles, extensors, flexors, and adductors was.53,.66,.45, and.59, respectively), suggesting that the relationship between UPF consumption and Goutallier grade was linear. Linear regression results from both the BMI-adjusted model (model 1) and the AC-adjusted model (model 2) showed a statistically significant relationship between UPF consumption and Goutallier grade for all muscles (model 1: β = 0.108 \[95% CI: 0.029, 0.186\], *P* =.007; model 2: β = 0.134 \[95% CI: 0.052, 0.215\], *P* =.001) ([Table 2](#tbl2)), indicating an increase in Goutallier grade of greater than 0.1 SD for each 1-SD greater UPF proportion in the daily diet.

### Interaction Analysis of the Effect of Sex

The interaction analyses examining the effect of sex on the relationships between UPF consumption and Goutallier grades for bilateral thigh muscles as well as individual muscle groups yielded no evidence of differences (*P* value range for the interaction analyses,.227–.995 for model 1 and.458–.785 for model 2), suggesting that the relationships between UPF consumption and Goutallier grades do not vary by sex.

### Sensitivity Analyses

When models 1 and 2 were additionally adjusted for total dietary fat, they showed statistically significant relationships between UPF consumption and Goutallier grades for adductors, flexors, and all thigh muscles (*P* value range,.001–.007) ([Table S1](#supplementary-materials)) similar to those of the original models. Results for extensors were statistically significant in the AC-adjusted model (*P* =.02) ([Table S1](#supplementary-materials)). Sensitivity analyses stratified by KL grade consistently showed stronger relationships between UPF consumption and Goutallier grade in knees with doubtful radiographic KOA (KL grade 1 \[*n* = 286 knees\]; standardized β range, 0.152–0.167 \[*P* range,.005–.007\] for flexors, adductors, and all thigh muscles in model 1; standardized β range, 0.124–0.186 \[*P* value range,.002–.045\] for flexors, extensors, adductors, and all thigh muscles in model 2) than in knees without radiographic KOA (KL grade 0 \[*n* = 885 knees\]; standardized β range, 0.093–0.116 \[*P* value range,.001–.005\] for flexors, adductors, and all thigh muscles in model 1; standardized β range, 0.087–0.149 \[*P* value range, <.001 to.008\] for flexors, extensors, adductors, and all thigh muscles in model 2) ([Table S2](#supplementary-materials)).

## Discussion

The relationship between high ultra-processed food (UPF) consumption and musculoskeletal health remains underexplored. Thus, we aimed to investigate the relationship between UPF consumption and thigh muscle fat infiltration (MFI) in a population at risk for knee osteoarthritis, using thigh MRI. In this cross-sectional secondary analysis using baseline data from 615 participants in the Osteoarthritis Initiative without symptomatic or radiographic osteoarthritis, we found that for every 1-SD greater UPF proportion in the diet, the sum Goutallier grade in all thigh muscles was about 0.1-SD greater (β = 0.108 \[95% CI: 0.029, 0.186\]; *P* =.007), indicating that poor diet quality is associated with greater thigh MFI, independent of sociodemographic features and dietary caloric intake. The relationship was more pronounced when the model was adjusted for abdominal circumference, a measure of obesity that also reflects fat distribution (β = 0.134 \[95% CI: 0.052, 0.215\]; *P* =.001), instead of body mass index.

Relationships between the incidence and progression of KOA and decline in quadriceps function, size, and quality were shown previously ([^10],[^28]). In our sample, the extensors were least affected by MFI. Their relative sparing from MFI could explain the weak relationship observed between UPF consumption and MFI in the extensors, rather than representing a selective relationship between diet and a specific muscle group.

An earlier study highlighted that abdominal obesity causes an anterior shift in the center of gravity, impairing postural stability and leading to functional limitation and slower gait in adults with KOA ([^29]). Our findings suggest that the impaired postural stability and functional limitation can be further exacerbated by greater MFI in the thigh muscles in relationship to UPF consumption—a known contributor to abdominal obesity ([^6]).

Although UPFs are substantial sources of energy, mainly due to added fat and sugar for increased palatability, they are inadequate sources of protein and essential nutrients ([^3]). A poor-quality diet lacking essential nutrients could impair muscle sustenance, leading to MFI. Skeletal muscle, which modulates energy and lipid metabolism, constitutes approximately 40% of total body weight and contains 50%–75% of all body proteins ([^30]). Examining muscle energetics and composition in older individuals with KOA, Distefano et al ([^28]) demonstrated greater MFI in those with moderate-severe KOA, indicating specific alterations in myocellular characteristics associated with the presence and severity of the disease. Furthermore, with advancing age, the body’s adipose tissue increases at the expense of muscle mass. This excess fat is stored in visceral and intramuscular compartments, which has inflammatory and functional implications ([^26]).

In our study, we applied semiquantitative Goutallier grading on T1-weighted spin-echo MRI scans to assess MFI in a 7.5-cm-long thigh section rather than relying on a single cross-sectional image. Goutallier grading is widely accepted in clinical practice for evaluating surgical candidates for rotator cuff repair ([^22],[^31]) and has had expanded use in research, including in evaluating thigh muscle structural changes in patients with amputation ([^32]). It has been validated against quantitative MRI sequences such as Lava Flex (GE HealthCare) and Ideal IQ (GE HealthCare) and MR spectroscopy. Without additional costly imaging or software requirements, it provides a quick volumetric assessment of the relative contractile components and visible fatty changes within a muscle ([^33],[^34]). It can be used in almost all clinical MRI studies, enabling further research on large populations to validate our findings.

By choosing a sample free of symptomatic or radiographic knee or hip osteoarthritis and systemic comorbidities, we aimed to eliminate potential confounders such as compromised muscle quality due to osteoarthritic joints, disuse-induced muscle atrophy, or chronic disease–associated muscle wasting. Yet, the sensitivity analyses suggest that the relationship between UPF consumption and MFI was stronger in the presence of doubtful radiographic disease (KL grade 1), underscoring the need to investigate the impact of diet quality in patients with overt KOA.

This study has several limitations. First, the Goutallier grading system has moderate reproducibility and does not provide robust quantitative measures of intramuscular fat or account for focal regions of inhomogeneous MFI ([^34]). Additionally, although aggregate scores from ordinal semiquantitative scales—such as the Whole-Organ Magnetic Resonance Imaging Score for KOA ([^35])—are widely used in research, they do not reflect equal intervals between categories, which may limit the interpretation of summed scores as a continuous composite measure compared with robust quantitative techniques. Second, we relied on results from a skewed population of aging adults at risk for KOA, who may have a higher risk for disability and frailty compared with younger adults. Although we adjusted for age in all analyses, the findings may not be fully generalizable to populations outside the age range and characteristics of the OAI participants. Third, despite our efforts to account for several potential confounders, we may not have fully captured all socioeconomic factors, lifestyle choices, or environmental exposures. Fourth, the FFQ was not primarily designed to assess the processing methods of culinary ingredients, requiring reliance on secondary use of existing data. It also predates the NOVA classification system, which itself exhibits low to moderate interrater agreement for assigning foods to individual NOVA groups ([^36]). Fifth, as the FFQ asked questions about the dietary habits of the participants over the past year, recall bias is likely, which could explain the lower-than-expected total daily caloric intake from the precalculated data based on the FFQ answers ([^37]). Sixth, because the FFQ was administered only once, we could not conduct a longitudinal assessment. Consequently, our results do not necessarily support causal interpretations and should be approached as “hypothesis generating,” and they require further validation in large-scale longitudinal studies using quantitative imaging data. Seventh, it is important to note the global increase in UPF consumption over the past 2 decades. UPFs constituted approximately 40% of our study participants’ annual diet on average, but recent data indicate that more than 50% of total energy in the modern American diet originates from UPFs ([^1]). Lastly, given that the growing use of glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists for weight control may contribute to sarcopenia, further investigation is required to understand the relationship between UPF consumption and muscle composition in patients using these medications. Thus, our results might not reflect the current relationship between diet and muscle quality.

In conclusion, our study provides valuable evidence linking ultra-processed food consumption to poor muscle quality, characterized by higher fat content on thigh MRI scans, regardless of sex, in individuals at risk for knee osteoarthritis. These findings have not been previously well established in the literature and lay the groundwork for future studies.
