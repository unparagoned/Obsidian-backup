## 1\. Introduction

Shorter telomere length has been associated with an increased risk of several age-related diseases (), and growing evidence indicates that individuals with MDD tend to exhibit shorter telomeres compared to non-depressed controls (). Whether MDD causes telomere attrition, or telomere attrition reflects environmental factors that are also linked to MDD is not currently known. Most prior research has relied on cross-sectional designs (), which often include both men and women without conducting sex-specific analyses. This limits interpretability, especially given that oestrogen fluctuations are closely linked to depression (), and oestrogens may play a protective role in telomere maintenance (; ). Moreover, since a substantial portion of inter-individual variability in telomere length is established at birth (), longitudinal data are better equipped for disentangling the influences of environmental risk factors on telomere length across the lifespan.

To address these limitations, we investigated genetic and environmental risk factors for depression and their associations with telomere dynamics in a large, population-based cohort of older UK women (). The non-clinical nature of this cohort allowed us to better disentangle the relationships of individual environmental risk factors (), such as socioeconomic status and health behaviours, longitudinally in community-living individuals. By leveraging repeated measures of telomere length, our study aimed to test whether depression (and its genetic liability) or its associated risk factors are also correlates of accelerated cellular ageing in women. As all environmental risk factors were measured prior to the first assessment of telomere length, we were able to explore the question while reducing potential reverse causality.

## 2\. Methods

### 2.1. Study Population

TwinsUK is an ongoing population-based twin registry originally established to study osteoporosis and osteoarthritis and has since expanded to investigate a wide range of genetic and environmental influences on health outcomes (). The cohort predominantly comprises middle-aged women (83 %), with 51 % monozygotic (MZ) and 49 % dizygotic (DZ) twins (). The Twins-UK study is approved by the St Thomas' Hospital Research Ethics Committee (REC reference: EC04/015) (). Full study details, including recruitment procedures and ethical approvals, are available at [https://twinsuk.ac.uk/researchers/explore-our-data-and-samples/](https://twinsuk.ac.uk/researchers/explore-our-data-and-samples/). The authors assert that all procedures contributing to this work comply with the ethical standards of the relevant national and institutional committees on human experimentation and with the Helsinki Declaration of 1975, as revised in 2008.

Among participants with valid telomere length data (described in the next section), there were 11 participants of mixed, other or unknown ethnic groups. Due to this small number, we restricted our analyses to participants of White ethnicity. As there were 29 only male participants, which precluded adequately powered subgroup analyses, we also excluded male participants. The distributions of the selected environmental factors and depression-related characteristics, based on participants with complete data for each variable, are presented in . Among 958 women, there were 89 MZ pairs, 215 DZ pairs, and 350 unrelated individuals. The median (IQR) age at first RTL measure was 58 (51–64) years (min.: 29; max.: 83) years. Further information of the definition and grouping of the selected environmental risk factors for the current analysis can be found in Supplementary Table 1. The years during which all variables were collected were summarised in Supplementary Table 2.

### 2.2. Telomere length measurement

New relative telomere length (RTL) in TwinsUK was generated using an established quantitative PCR protocol () as utilised by our lab previously (), following key quality control criteria as outlined by the Telomere Research Network. To assess reproducibility, we repeated a set of 92 samples using this method on separate days, revealing an intraclass correlation coefficient of 0.919 \[95 % CI: 0.878–0.946, *P* = 1.29 × 10 <sup>−37</sup>\]. Measures exceeding 3.5 standard deviations from the overall mean were excluded. Further details of telomere length measurements and quality control procedures are detailed in Supplementary information S1.

As telomeres were measured in white blood cells, we examined a subset of participants with available blood cell composition data. We found no statistical evidence that any tested blood cell type composition was associated with telomere length (*p* > 0.05; Supplementary Information S2 and Supplementary Table 3).

We only used accessed RTL data from DNA samples collected on/after 28th May 2004 due to laboratory protocol changes. This ensured all blood samples were extracted using the same DNA extraction method (Nucleon BACC3 Genomic DNA extraction kit, Cytiva, Little Chalfont, UK). As depression-related variables were collected mostly in 2006, we only used RTL measures collected in/after 2007 for the corresponding analyses. The final analytical dataset included 958 women, with 162 having one measurement, 675 having two measures, 119 having three, and two having four measures available. The key aims of the current study were to consider the relationships of environmental and genetic risk factors for depression on telomere length and telomere attrition, .

### 2.3. Telomere attrition estimation

Telomere attrition was defined as the rate of change in RTL over years of follow-up estimated using linear mixed models of repeated measures, as described below. To test whether attrition trajectories varied by age, we modelled the non-linear effects of attained age using linear spline models with one, two and three knots, separately. We compared the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) of the linear and non-linear models. More information is available in Supplementary Information on telomere length measures over follow-up.

### 2.4. Depression and antidepressant use

For the analyses of depression and of antidepressant use with telomere length, we used depression-related data collected in or before 2006 and restricted the sample to women with telomere length measurements available from 2007. Depression status was defined using self-reported ever clinical diagnosis collected during 2004–2006. Ever antidepressant use was derived from three questions related to self-reported antidepressant use asked during 2004–2006. In 2006, participants were asked if they have had suffered from depression or had a clinical diagnosis of depression, along with whether they had been treated with antidepressants. This formed an additional analysis in which antidepressant use among depressed patients was examined in relation to baseline telomere length. All depression-related variables were dichotomous.

### 2.5. Polygenic score analyses

Although various methods of PGS development are available, we focused on more recent publications of PGS scores derived from GWAS conducted in samples of predominantly European ancestry. While no single PGS method outperforms all others, LDpred2 () and MegaPRS () demonstrated better performance for different traits (). We chose one from LDpred2 or MegaPRS if available (Supplementary Table 4) to avoid unnecessary multiple testing burden. Details of phenotype and genotype quality control in TwinsUK can be found in Supplementary information S3. We included 707 women in this polygenic score analysis.

### 2.6. Environmental risk factors

Environmental exposures were collected from questionnaire data, summarised in , and our approach depicted in . These are grouped into early-life factors (birthweight, exposure to child abuse), socioeconomic status (highest educational attainment, mother's highest qualification, father's highest qualification, Index of Multiple Deprivation), health behaviours (ever smoking, current smoking status, current alcohol use, physical activity), adiposity (waist-to-hip ratio, body mass index), and dietary patterns (scores for fruit and vegetables, high alcohol, traditional English, low meat, and dieting). In the analyses, only environmental factors measured prior to the first RTL measure (from 28th May 2004) were investigated in order to minimise reverse causation bias. Most behavioural and anthropometric risk factors were collected from questionnaires or assessments conducted between 1997 and 1999 (Supplementary table 2). For early-life and socioeconomic factors, we allowed data collection years beyond 2004 as these variables were assumed to be either historical, relatively stable over time or time-invariant. Therefore, our analyses assess whether a single measure of health-related variables prior to baseline RTL measures are associated with the baseline RTL and/or its subsequent rate of change during follow-up.

## 3\. Statistical analyses

### 3.1. Associations of depression-related and environmental factors with telomere length

Associations between environmental exposures and baseline RTL (i.e., first available RTL measure) were assessed using linear mixed models. These models included random intercepts for family ID (to account for within-family clustering) and qPCR plate and row position to account for laboratory batch effects. Fixed effects included were age at the RTL measure, zygosity (MZ or DZ), and the exposure variable of interest. To assess the robustness of our results to the inclusion of related individuals, we conducted sensitivity analyses whereby we randomly selected only one twin from each twin pair and repeated the analyses. The conclusions remained unchanged (results not shown).

### 3.2. Associations of depression-related and environmental factors with telomere attrition

Telomere attrition was defined as the rate of change in RTL during follow-up estimated using linear mixed models incorporating all available RTL timepoints. The outcome of the linear mixed model was RTL. The model included random intercepts for participant ID (to account for repeated measures from the same individual) nested within family ID, qPCR plate, and row position. Fixed effects included were “time since RTL measurement” (hereafter called “time”), zygosity, age at first RTL measure, and the exposure variable, with the coefficient of the interaction term of “time” by exposure being the coefficient for hypothesis testing. We tested health-related exposures prior to the first baseline assessment of RTL.

### 3.3. Associations of polygenic risk scores on telomere length and attrition

Linear mixed models were used to examine the associations between PGS and either baseline standardised RTL or standardised telomere attrition, adjusting for the first five principal components of ancestry and zygosity and including a random intercept for family. For baseline telomere length, we further adjusted for baseline age, qPCR plate and row number. For the linear mixed models of attrition rate, we calculated the difference between the final and baseline measurements, divided by the time interval between the two measures (in years), to simplify modelling. Based on two time points, a negative value indicates telomere attrition, and a positive value indicates lengthening. Each disease-specific PGS was standardised using the mean and SD of the score in the European ancestry-matched reference population provided by GenoPred. Further adjustment for baseline age for the analyses of telomere attrition did not change the conclusion of the findings. More details can be found in Supplementary information S3. The analytical samples were 707 women, including 151 DZ and 66 MZ twin pairs.

We performed population-based PGS analyses as the primary set of analyses and conducted within-family PGS analyses as a sensitivity check. For the population-based PGS analyses that were statistically significant, we examined if the directions of associations were consistent for the between-family and within-family estimates, as a way to assess potential genetic confounding.

### 3.4. Multiple testing correction

Linear mixed analyses were conducted using lme4 and lmerTest packages (; ) in R 4.3.0 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria). For categorical environmental factors, we reported the estimates with 95 % confidence intervals (95 % CI) and corresponding *p* -values (P (ind)) for each category, along with the global p-value for hypothesis testing of that factor. All statistical tests were two-sided, with a significance threshold of 5 %. Both uncorrected p-values and false discovery rate (FDR) adjusted p-values are reported.

## 4\. Results

In 958 women, the median interval between the first and the last RTL measures ranged between 5 and 17 years (median \[IQR\]: 6 \[5–6\] years) (more details in Supplementary Table 5). Cross-sectionally, using the first RTL measure only older participants had shorter RTL on average (estimate: -0.003 \[95 % CI: −0.0054 to −0.0006\]).

Longitudinal analyses incorporating all repeated measures were used to estimate within-individual telomere changes (*N* = 958) and to separate this out from between-individual age differences. In these models, older age at baseline was associated with shorter RTL overall (estimate: −0.005; 95 % CI: −0.006 to −0.003), while follow-up time (years) was associated with a decline in RTL within individuals (estimate: −0.016; 95 % CI: −0.019 to −0.013). As the mean RTL at baseline age (58 years) was estimated to be 1.19 in this model, the mean annual telomere attrition rate was about 1.3 % (dividing the coefficient of follow-up time on RTL \[i.e., −0.016\] by the mean RTL at baseline age \[i.e., 1.19\]). When non-linear effects of attained age were tested using linear spline models, the linear model, with no age knots, had the lowest BIC of 577, compared to one (BIC = 579, knot at 61 years), two (BIC = 587) and three knots (BIC = 591). This suggests that the relationship between attained age and telomere length is best represented by a linear model. More results of the descriptive analyses can be found in Supplementary Information S4 and Supplementary Table 6 and 7.

### 4.1. Depression and antidepressant use

When restricting the analysis to RTL measures from 2007 onward, the remaining sample included 889 women, of whom 640 had two RTL measurements and 66 had three. We first examined the association between lifetime depression status and RTL. Reporting depression was not significantly associated with RTL, though trended towards a negative effect (estimate: -0.044, 95 % CI: −0.089 to 0.002, *p*  = 0.06). Among the 245 participants who reported ever having depression, 91 reported current use of antidepressants in the same questionnaire. To disentangle the effects of depression from antidepressant use, we performed a sensitivity analysis restricted to participants who either did not report ever having depression or who reported both ever having depression and current use of antidepressants. Compared to those who never reported depression (*n*  = 551), the coefficient for participants who reported both ever depression and current use of antidepressants in relation to baseline RTL was −0.09 (95 % CI: −0.158 to −0.022, *p*  = 0.01) while the coefficient for rate of change in RTL was 0.007 (−0.005 to 0.019, *p* = 0.24).

### 4.2. Environmental risk factors for depression

Among the environmental risk factors analysed, waist-to-hip ratio (WHR) was the only variable associated with telomere attrition. Higher WHR ratio was associated with greater telomere shortening (estimate: -0.005 \[95 % CI: −0.008 to −0.001\], uncorrected p = 0.01). Whilst it survived multiple testing within its category of adiposity, it did not survive when considering all environmental variables tested, . We conducted a sensitivity analysis for WHR to assess the impact on additional adjustment for BMI and its interaction with time, but the association remained statistically significant (*p*  = 0.03). We also performed a within pair association test between WHR and RTL in 200 twin pairs. We decomposed the crude WHR into a twin pair mean and a twin pair difference. We calculated rate of change in telomere length based on the first and last available RTL and regressed that on these two variables. Higher average WHR was associated with more attrition per year (−0.083 \[−0.19 to 0.03\], *p*  = 0.14), and within the twin pairs, the twin with a higher WHR also showed more attrition per year (−0.10 \[−0.26 to 0.05\], *p*  = 0.18). However, neither of the estimates were statistically significant. The coefficients of twin pair mean and twin pair difference in WHR were not statistically significantly different (chi-square = 0.05, *p* = 0.82). Twin modelling showed no evidence that genetic/shared environmental components explain the association between WHR and attrition rate in RTL. In a subset of 296 participants with a repeated measures of waist and hip circumferences at a median 2.9 years later (IQR, 2.4–4 years), the correlation of waist and hip circumferences were 0.83 and 0.77 respectively.

Other factors, including educational attainment, Index of Multiple Deprivation, smoking status, alcohol consumption, physical activity, childhood abuse, birthweight, and parental education, showed no significant associations with baseline RTL or telomere attrition (Supplementary tables 8 and 9).

### 4.3. Polygenic risk for major depression and comorbid age-related diseases

No consistent associations were observed between PGS for MDD and either baseline RTL or telomere attrition. Similarly, we largely found no evidence of associations between genetic liability to depression and its common comorbidities and telomere length or telomere attrition rate ().

PGS for coronary artery disease was associated with shorter telomere length (*p* = 0.02) but not with telomere attrition rates, and the within and between family estimates were directionally consistent with the main finding (Supplementary table 10).

## 5\. Discussion

In this study, we investigated the associations of depression, its genetic liability, and a range of environmental/health risk factors with both baseline telomere length and telomere attrition in a large, community living sample of adult UK women. Our longitudinal analyses, capturing RTL measures with an average of six years, allowed us to measure cumulative attrition of telomere length with age independent of the difference in baseline telomere length (much of the variation attributable to differences established at birth).

We found no statistically significant associations between self-reported depression and telomere length or attrition, although there was a trend towards shorter telomeres in those reporting depression (*p*  = 0.06), consistent with previous literature. Notably, we observed a nominal association between antidepressant use and shorter telomere length (*p* = 0.02), replicating our earlier findings in other UK community-based samples (). While prior studies have suggested that this association may reflect confounding by depression severity (), the consistency of the finding across cohorts raises the possibility that antidepressant use itself could be linked to telomere dynamics, potentially via biological pathways such as increased cellular turnover or metabolic side effects. However, this association did not hold within the smaller subset of individuals reporting depression, nor did we observe an association longitudinally, suggesting caution in interpretation. A limitation of the depression analyses is that we did not have data to assess the concordance between self-reported GP/psychiatric depression and adjudicated clinical diagnoses. However, our consistent findings related to antidepressant use warrants further investigation.

When considering genetic risk factors, we found no significant association between PGS for MDD and telomere length. This finding aligns with our previous results () but contrasts with associations reported in the UK Biobank (), likely reflecting their substantially larger sample size and the small effect sizes involved. Importantly, our study is the first to assess whether genetic risk influences telomere attrition, providing a novel longitudinal perspective on potential dynamic effects. Although no significant associations emerged, several observations warrant discussion.

First, coronary artery disease (CAD) is frequently comorbid with depression, and prior cross-sectional work by () demonstrated evidence that shorter telomeres causally increase CAD risk. Consistent with this, our PGS analysis showed a negative direction of effect between genetic risk for CAD and telomere length. However, we observed no evidence (or trend) of an association between CAD genetic risk and telomere attrition. This discrepancy may suggest a unidirectional relationship, whereby telomere length influences CAD risk but not vice versa. Given that much of telomere length variability is established early in life, it is also possible that the genetic variants shaping baseline telomere length differ from those influencing its decline over time.

Second, we detected a borderline positive association between the within-family PGS for type-2 diabetes and rate of change in telomere length. Whilst potentially interesting, this finding should be interpreted with caution due to the large standard error. Given the modest predictive power of PGS in our sample, replication in larger cohorts will be essential to determine whether this reflects true pleiotropy or simply a chance result.

Findings presented so far provide limited evidence for consistent associations between depression, or genetic risk factors on telomere dynamics, highlighting the need to investigate environmental factors as potential upstream moderators of biological pathways relevant to both telomere length and mental health. We examined a broad range of environmental factors, including early-life adversity, health behaviours, adiposity, dietary patterns, and socioeconomic status. Our cross-sectional findings differ from those reported in the UK Biobank, where modest associations between telomere length and several environmental variables were observed (health behaviours including smoking, physical activity, diet, body weight and alcohol intake together explained <0.2 % variation in RTL in a sample size of >420,000) (). Therefore, this discrepancy may be due to limited statistical power. Notably, among the longitudinal analyses in our study, waist-to-hip ratio, a measure of central adiposity, appeared to be associated with accelerated telomere attrition. Visceral obesity is linked to chronic inflammation () and oxidative stress (), both of which are suggested mechanisms driving leukocyte telomere shortening. Importantly, over half of the waist and hip circumference measurements in our study were collected nearly a decade before the first telomere measurement, supporting a temporal relationship between visceral adiposity and subsequent accelerated telomere attrition. However, we found little evidence for an association between body mass index and telomere attrition, or body mass index with baseline telomere length, which contrasts with meta-analysis findings in the literature (). There is possibility that central fat distribution, rather than overall adiposity, is more biologically relevant in shaping telomere dynamics.

Given our all-female sample, we also considered the potential influence of hormonal changes during menopause, as oestrogens are hypothesised to slow telomere attrition via telomerase activation (). However, our linear spline analyses did not reveal non-linear attrition patterns across the late menopausal and postmenopausal age range (ages 50–65 years). While we lacked individual-level data on menopausal status, which limited our ability to test the oestrogen-telomerase activation hypothesis, our analysis suggests that hormonal fluctuations during this period may not impact on telomere dynamics.

While previous studies employing Mendelian randomisation found an association between higher educational attainment and longer RTL (), our analyses of socioeconomic variables did not support this, possibly due to lower statistical power, as reflected by the wider 95 % confidence intervals compared with other behavioural variables. Previously published MR studies on education may also be biased by demographic and indirect genetic factors (; ), as observed in associations with BMI, CRP, and height. Triangulating evidence using multiple methods and in larger studies is required to clarify any associations with small-to-moderate effect sizes.

Despite the important insights, this study also has limitations. First, telomere length was measured using quantitative PCR, albeit in accordance with best practices outlined by the Telomere Research Network. Although this approach demonstrated high reproducibility in our data, inherent measurement error of the qPCR method (; ) is a limitation and may have reduced the statistical power to detect modest associations between risk factors and telomere attrition. Second, due to the small number of male and non-European participants, we excluded these individuals from our analyses to maintain statistical robustness. As a result, our sample predominantly comprises middle-aged White women, which limits the generalisability of our findings to other sex, age, and ethnic groups. Future large-scale longitudinal studies with repeated telomere measures across more diverse populations are needed to clarify modifiable risk factors for biological ageing.

Third, there were limitations related to our modelling and choices of exposures. For instance, most participants had only two measures of RTL, which has some impact on estimating the random slope of time and the precision of longitudinal trajectories. Additionally, in the case of adverse childhood experiences, we lacked data from validated measures such as those from the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire, and relied on shorter self-report questionnaire responses (though we followed the best practice approach in our sample, as detailed elsewhere ()). Furthermore, for some variables we grouped participants who answered “preferred not to answer” into the “no” category, which may introduce misclassification. However, this misclassification is likely to have biased the results towards the null. Moreover, as with other association analyses involving time-varying exposure (such as BMI and alcohol intake), the use of a single baseline measure can introduce regression dilution bias, which would attenuate the true association but does not inflate estimates. However, in the case of our significant association with WHR, the short-term repeatability of waist and hip circumferences were high (r ∼ 0.8), suggesting that our coefficients were not substantially underestimated.

In conclusion, we observed telomere attrition in white blood cells across mid-to-late life in women. We found limited evidence that depression, its genetic liability, or most environmental exposures were robustly associated with telomere dynamics. Antidepressant use was nominally associated with shorter telomere length. Waist-to-hip ratio emerged as a potential modifiable risk factor, highlighting the possible relevance of central adiposity to cellular ageing. The hypothesised link between psychiatric disorders and accelerated biological ageing via telomere maintenance remains complex. Mechanistically informed studies and intervention-focused studies are needed to understand what modifications could meaningfully improve the lives of depressed patients, promote healthier ageing, and ultimately reduce the chances of patients also developing age-related diseases.
