## 1\. Introduction

Stress, anxiety, and depression are common psychological distress (). According to the World Health Organization (WHO), the global prevalence of anxiety and depression has recently increased by a substantial 25 % (). Recent systematic review and meta-analysis revealed that, in Saudi Arabia almost one third suffered from depression in particular among older adults (; ). Women have higher incidence of depression comparting to men. (;; ).

Additionally, anxiety is common in Saudi Arabia, a study involving 1160 participants found that 24 % of them suffered from moderate-to-severe anxiety and 22.3 % from moderate-to-severe stress (). Studies have found that the rates of stress and anxiety are higher among females, young people, and health practitioners (;; ).

Moreover, according to Harvard Health Publishing, 50 % of adults with anxiety have a sleep disorder. In Saudi Arabia poor sleep with short duration is common. Short sleep duration <7 hours/night is very prevalent in KSA, it affects one in every 3 Saudi adults (; ).

A study found that 72.5 % out of 433 adults, has poor sleep. The highest percentage of poor sleepers was found among those aged 18–28 years (). A recent study reported that out of 457 participants, 25 % had poor sleep quality, with higher prevalence among women (). Additionally, higher prevalence of severe insomnia was observed among younger participants, obese participants, and university educated participants, and those who practiced exercise four times or more weekly ().

Anxiety may contribute to nightmares or insomnia, and sleep deprivation can also elevate the risk of an individual developing an anxiety disorder (; ). Additionally, sleep disorders are considered to be one of the core symptoms of depression (; ).

Sleep disorders and poor sleep quality are common and are directly associated with a wide variety of physical and mental health difficulties (). Sleep deprivation is linked to larger memory impairment (; ).

Additionally, they can influence behaviors like overeating and smoking (; ). Psychological distress can lead to an increase in heart rate, elevation of blood pressure, and release of stress hormones. These changes might lead to increasing the risk of heart attack, sudden cardiac death, heart failure, or arrhythmias (). Additionally, more consequences of long-term anxiety and stress include muscle tension, an increased risk of coronary disease, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), peptic ulcers, gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), and overall life impairment. Thus, it is important to treat stress, anxiety, and sleep disorders to prevent their negative effects on physical health (;; ).

Mental disorders such as stress and anxiety can occur at any time and at any age, and environmental factors might increase an individual's risk of developing one. Social media-induced mental health problems is directly proportional to the amount of time spent on these sites, the frequency of usage, and the number of platforms being used. Various possible explanations have been implicated, including a negative impact on self-esteem through unhealthy comparisons, social media burnout, stress, lack of emotional regulation due to social media preoccupation, and development of social anxiety due to decreased real-life social interactions. It has been postulated that increased social media use is driven by preexisting anxiety and is a coping strategy (; ).

Additionally, fears of illness, death, or disability, as well as the fear of losing one's job, affected individuals' psychological state. One report also indicated that excessive follow-up of news on social media and television exacerbated anxiety ().

Moreover, diet might play a role in the development of mental disorders and sleep quality. Low intake of omega-3 has a negative impact on sleep, causing tiredness and sleep disorders (; ). Low intake of omega-3 has been associated with low levels of attention and alertness, memory problems, irritability and anxiety (; ). Additionally, a connection has been found between omega-3 consumption and a reduction in the information-processing problems seen in people with schizophrenia, obsessive–compulsive disorder, Huntington's disease, attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder, bipolar disorder, Alzheimer's disease, and other nervous system malfunctions (;; ). A key finding is that omega-3 fatty acids, such as docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) and eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA), may have a beneficial effect on nervous system function because they help to maintain the cell membranes of nerve cells. While 90 % of DHA is found in the brain, EPA is principally involved in the retina and nervous system. Low consumption of DHA impairs an individual's response to sensory stimuli, making them less calm and stable than others, and just a small decrement in the level of DHA in the brain can cause brain malfunction. In older adults, lower levels of DHA in the blood have been associated with a smaller brain size, a sign of accelerated brain aging ().

It has been reported in several studies that omega-3 can help with mental diseases, particularly depression, but it has no effect on those with a normal mood state (; ). In the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, omega-3 consumption consider low and there is lack of knowledge about the importance and role of omega-3 for human health (;;; ).

However, to our knowledge, no studies have been conducted among the Saudi population to measure the effect of omega-3 supplementation on depression, stress, anxiety, everyday memory, and sleep quality. Therefore, the aim of the present study was to determine the effects of three months of omega-3 consumption on these factors.

## 2\. Materials and Methods

Participants without chronic physical illness but with elevated psychological distress were recruited to participate in a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study. The inclusion criteria required that participants had not taken any omega-3 supplements for the last 6 months and had a high level of stress and anxiety, were permitted to be included. Exclusion criteria were pregnant and breastfeeding women and people with low psychological distress. The included participants were allocated to either the intervention (I) group or the control (C) group in a 1:1 ratio, using computer-generated random numbers. The intervention (I) group received omega-3 capsules at a concentration of 1000 mg/day (NOW Foods Ultra Omega-3500 EPA/250 DHA), while the control (C) group received corn oil capsules (400 mg/d). The placebo capsules were identical in appearance to the omega-3 capsules to ensure blinding. All participants received one capsule/day for three months.

Validated Arabic versions of the Perceived Stress Scale-10 (PSS-10) (), General Anxiety Disorder-7(GAD-7) (; ), Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) (), Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) (), Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS) (), and Everyday Memory Questionnaire (EMQ) (). were administered at baseline (week 0) and post-intervention (week 12). Conventional cut-offs were used: GAD-7 (0–4 minimal, 5–9 mild, 10–14 moderate, 15–21 severe), PHQ-9 (0–4 minimal, 5–9 mild, 10–14 moderate, 15–19 moderately severe, 20–27 severe), PSQI >5 indicating poor sleep quality, and ESS >10 indicating excessive daytime sleepiness. Where descriptive categories are reported in Results (e.g., “low/moderate/high”), they correspond to these thresholds unless otherwise specified.

Participants for the study were recruited by placing an advertisement. The advertisement includes a study description and contact details for the researchers. The advertisement was placed in public locations like cafes, university cafes and halls and online in different social media platforms.

To minimise bias, 64 participants were randomly assigned to their study group using a computer-generated sequence with variable block sizes. Allocation was implemented by an independent researcher who was not involved in enrolment, outcome assessment, or data analysis. Supplements were provided monthly, and adherence was monitored through daily WhatsApp self-check-ins and monthly in-person visits to count unused capsules. Adherence was calculated as the proportion of capsules taken relative to the total expected over 12 weeks. Participants were instructed to report any adverse events during weekly WhatsApp check-ins. No major adverse events were reported throughout the study.

### 2.1. Participant flow

Of the 380 individuals screened, ineligible or non-consenting individuals were excluded for not meeting the symptom thresholds, declining participation, or other reasons (). Sixty-four eligible participants consented and were randomized; all completed both baseline and post-intervention assessments and were included in the per-protocol analysis ().

#### 2.1.1. Primary outcome

The primary endpoint was the change in perceived stress (PSS) from baseline to week 12.

#### 2.1.2. Secondary outcomes

Secondary endpoints included changes in anxiety (GAD-7), depressive symptoms (PHQ-9), sleep quality (PSQI), daytime sleepiness (ESS), and everyday memory (EMQ).

#### 2.1.3. Multiple comparisons

No multiplicity correction was applied, as the trial was exploratory; results for secondary outcomes are interpreted as exploratory.

#### 2.1.4. Sample size

No formal a priori power calculation was performed. This study was designed as an exploratory randomized controlled trial, and a target sample size of 64 participants (32 per group) was chosen based on feasibility and in line with previous omega-3 intervention trials evaluating psychological or sleep outcomes, which have used comparable samples of approximately 50–70 participants for 12 weeks (;; ).

### 2.2. Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were performed using the statistical software package SPSS version 24. The data are presented as the mean, standard deviation (SD), and standard error of the mean (SEM) for descriptive statistics (PHQ-9, GAD-7, PSS, PSQI, ESS, and memory). To assess the changes within each group (control and intervention), a paired *t* -test was performed to compare pre- and post-intervention scores. An independent t-test was performed to compare the post-intervention scores between the two groups. Additionally, Linear regression models were used to assess between-group differences in post-intervention scores while adjusting for baseline values of the same measure to improve precision. This approach (ANCOVA) is commonly recommended even when baseline differences are not statistically significant, as it accounts for residual variability and increases statistical power.

Cohen's d effect size was calculated to measure the magnitude of the difference between the means of control and intervention groups in terms of standard deviations. Value of d = 0.2 for a small effect, d = 0.5 for a moderate effect, and d = 0.8 for a large effect (). A *p* -value of <0.05 was considered significant.

As this was an exploratory randomized trial, no formal multiplicity correction (e.g., Bonferroni) was applied. Secondary outcomes are therefore interpreted as exploratory.

Data were analysed using a per-protocol approach. Participants who completed both baseline and post-intervention assessments were included in the final analysis. Missing questionnaire items (<5 %) were imputed using series mean substitution; participants with >20 % missing data were excluded.

The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and approved by the Institutional Review Board (the Biomedical Ethics Committee at Um Al-Qura University, Makkah City (HAPO-02-K-012-2021-11-832)). The participants were provided written informed consent and asked to sign it.

## 4\. Discussion

The aim of this study was to determine the effects of omega-3 consumption on stress levels, anxiety, depression, everyday memory, and sleeping quality among Saudi population. The results indicate significant improvements in the psychological distress scores after 3 months of 500 EPA/250 DHA omega-3 supplementation in people with severe stress and anxiety. These findings support the hypothesis that omega-3 fatty acids exert beneficial effects on psychological wellbeing, particularly in relation to stress, anxiety, and sleep quality.

A statistically significant reduction in stress levels was observed, with a notable difference between the intervention and control groups. However, these findings contrast with those of a previous Australian randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial, which investigated the effect of omega-3 supplementation on chronic psychological stress. That study, which administered a higher dosage of 2.2 g of EPA and 0.11 g of DHA over a similar time frame, found no significant effect on perceived stress (). In contrast, another study involving 50 outpatients diagnosed with major depressive disorder (68 % female) demonstrated that omega-3 PUFA supplementation (1000 mg/day) as an adjunct to standard treatment (sertraline) significantly improved symptoms of depression and anxiety, sleep disturbances, and emotional regulation compared to a placebo ().

Furthermore, omega-3 helps to improve sleep quality significantly in the current study. This has been observed recently () and a systemic review confirms this finding, as omega-3 improved sleep quality in 74 % of the included clinical research (). This might be because it plays a role in the secretion of serotonin, which is a neurotransmitter that aids in sleep regulation (). Moreover, it is involved in the regulation of melatonin composition, which plays an important role in the onset and maintenance of sleep ().

On the other hand, there was no significant effect on the day sleepiness. This disagreement between these two scales has been reported recently in a systemic review (). Additionally, ESS reflects stable trait-level sleepiness, not acute night-to-night variability (; ). ESS also, can be influenced by gender and other physiological factors or daily habits, such as the use of electronic devices or caffeine consumption, which are not always directly linked to emotional disorders (;; ). The supplementation in this study enhance emotional disorders such as anxiety, depression, and stress, which will affect overall sleep quality more than daytime sleepiness. This might be due to how these emotions influence the brain's nighttime processes. During sleep, the brain regulates emotions and processes emotional information, which can be disrupted by these disorders. This leads to decreased sleep quality, such as interruptions or difficulty falling asleep, but does not necessarily cause daytime sleepiness, which is more related to factors such as total sleep duration or specific conditions such as sleep apnea ().

In the present study, everyday memory was significantly improved, with a large effect size, after the intervention, indicating the clinical importance of the use of omega-3. A systematic review concluded that the ingestion of omega-3 fatty acids enhances learning, memory, cognitive wellbeing, and blood flow in the brain. Omega-3 treatment is advantageous, well-tolerated, and risk-free (). Additionally, a study of 2183 dementia- and stroke-free participants found that a higher omega-3 index was associated with larger hippocampal volumes. The hippocampus, a structure in the brain, plays a major role in learning and memory ().

Omega-3 promotes cognition, neuronal preservation, and protection against neurodegeneration (). In the current study there was a significant improvement in everyday memory. There are two possible mechanisms that play a role in neurotransmission: changing membrane fluidity and increasing neurotransmitter release (). Omega-3 also alleviates brain apoptosis through a number of mechanisms, such as by decreasing responses to reactive oxygen species, which have an anti-apoptotic action, or by up-regulating the expression of anti-apoptotic proteins and down-regulating the expression of apoptotic proteins, slowing down apoptosis ().

These findings may have important clinical implications. Given the favorable safety profile and accessibility of omega-3 supplements, this intervention could be considered as a complementary strategy alongside conventional therapies (; ). Notably, the dose used in this study (500 mg of EPA and 250 mg of DHA) is both practical and well-tolerated, making it a feasible option in everyday clinical practice (). However, individual responses to omega-3 supplementation may vary depending on factors such as baseline omega-3 status, dietary habits, inflammation levels, and genetic variability (). Emerging research suggests that individuals with lower baseline omega-3 indices or higher systemic inflammation may derive greater psychological benefits from omega-3 supplementation (; ). For instance, a randomized controlled trial found that higher doses of omega-3 fatty acids improved motivational symptoms and cognition in depressed individuals exhibiting a pro-inflammatory phenotype (). This highlights the potential for a more tailored, precision-nutrition approach when considering omega-3 s as part of mental health management.

Although, our findings agree with previously reported omega-3 trials. This may be explained by characteristics of our sample, which consisted of individuals with severe psychological distress. For instant, the positive effect of high omeg-3 dosage (≥1 mg) are stronger in those with clinical conditions compared to subclinical populations (; ). Saudi adults also have particularly low habitual omega-3 intake and low baseline omega-3 status (), which is associated with stronger treatment responses (). Adherence in this study was high due to daily monitoring, a factor known to enhance the observed efficacy of omega-3 supplementation. () Moreover, the EPA-dominant formulation used here is particularly effective for mood-related outcomes compared with DHA-dominant preparations (;;; ).

There are several limitations in the current study. Firstly, the number of female participants was higher than the number of male participants; secondly, there was no clinical measurement of relevant stress hormones. Secondly, the reliance on self-reported adherence and outcome measures. Although daily WhatsApp check-ins and monthly capsule counts were conducted to promote compliance, objective biomarkers such as plasma omega-3 levels were not assessed. Additionally, all psychological and behavioural outcomes were measured through self-reported instruments, which may introduce reporting bias. On the other hand, this study also has several strengths. Firstly, it was a randomized, double-blind trial. Secondly, the dosages of both EPA and DHA (500EPA/250DHA) were higher than those used in other studies, and this might have had a pronounced effect. Finally, the trial took place over 3 continuous months, and the participants were followed up daily by the researchers to ensure that they were consuming the supplements.

## 5\. Conclusions

This study has demonstrated that the consumption of 1000 mg/day of omega-3 (500 EPA/250 DHA) for 3 months reduced psychological stress and enhanced everyday memory and sleep quality in Saudi people with psychological distress. Further studies should be conducted to measure the effects of this dose of omega-3 on individuals with other mental disorders. In future studies, a larger dose could be considered, and blood tests could be conducted to measure the levels of adrenaline, triglycerides, and sugar. Additionally, future studies should incorporate validated cognitive performance tests and explore the long-term effects of omega-3 intake, particularly in populations at higher risk of cognitive decline. Additionally, stratifying participants by age, sex, and baseline dietary omega-3 intake may further elucidate the specific subgroups who stand to benefit the most.
