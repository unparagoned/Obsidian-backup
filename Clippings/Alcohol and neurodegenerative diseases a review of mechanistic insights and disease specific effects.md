## ABSTRACT

*Background:* Neurodegenerative diseases including Alzheimer’s disease (AD), Parkinson’s disease (PD), Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (Also), and Huntington’s disease (HD) represent a significant global public health problem. Alcohol consumption is a common lifestyle factor that has been implicated as both a risk factor and potential modifier of disease progression.

*Objectives:* This review integrates evidence from human and experimental studies to characterize the effects of alcohol consumption on the onset and progression of major neurodegenerative diseases.

*Methods:* A narrative review was undertaken examining the pathophysiological effects of alcohol on the brain and its disease-specific effects on neurodegenerative disorders, integrating findings from human cohort studies and mechanistic investigations in preclinical models.

*Results:* Experimental evidence indicates that chronic alcohol consumption exacerbates neurodegeneration through multiple converging mechanisms, including oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, lipid peroxidation, inflammatory signaling, disruption of neurotrophic pathways, impairment of dopaminergic neurotransmission, and alcohol-induced gut microbiota dysbiosis with blood-brain barrier compromise. Epidemiological data suggest dose-dependent and disease-specific associations, with heavy and sustained consumption more consistently linked to increased risk or accelerated progression of AD and PD, while evidence in Also and HD remains inconsistent.

*Conclusion:* Alcohol exerts a multifaceted and context-dependent influence on neurodegenerative diseases. Accumulating evidence supports that long-term heavy alcohol consumption is associated with enhanced neurodegeneration. Minimizing alcohol consumption may present a pragmatic opportunity to reduce neurodegenerative risk.

## Introduction

Neurodegenerative diseases constitute a heterogeneous group of progressive disorders with significant medical, social and economic impacts worldwide ([Citation1](#), [Citation2](#)). Despite great diversity in epidemiology, neuropathology, and genetics, Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is currently the most common cause of dementia worldwide ([Citation3](#)), and Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disease ([Citation4](#)). Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (Also) and Huntington’s disease (HD), in contrast, are much less frequent with much lower rates of incidence and prevalence ([Citation5](#), [Citation6](#)), but are highly disabling and invariably fatal, representing an enormous clinical burden.

Neurodegenerative diseases are sporadic and recognized as resulting from the interplay of genetic predisposition and chronic accumulation of environmental influences ([Citation7](#)). Among these, alcohol consumption represents one of the most prevalent and modifiable environmental risk factors worldwide. Importantly, alcohol-related neurotoxicity exhibits a clear dose-dependent profile. Based on contemporary consensus definitions, moderate consumption is defined as approximately 2 drinks per day for men and 1 drink per day for women, whereas heavy consumption is defined as ≥ 15 drinks per week for men and ≥8 drinks per week for women, with thresholds varying slightly by sex and population ([Citation8](#)). Emerging evidence suggests that cumulative lifetime alcohol exposure is an important predictor of the magnitude and persistence of alcohol-related neural damage, a conclusion recently supported by the National Institutes of Health/National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (NIH/NIAAA) ([Citation9](#)). Chronic alcohol use leads to structural brain atrophy, disruption of excitatory-inhibitory neurotransmission, and accelerated neuronal damage through excitotoxicity and oxidative stress. These neurotoxic events are frequently exacerbated by alcohol-related nutritional deficiencies, which contribute to cognitive impairment and increase the risk of dementia ([Citation10](#)). Epidemiological studies confirm a significant association between heavy alcohol consumption and neurodegenerative diseases. In a large-scale French cohort study conducted between 2008 and 2013, alcohol use disorder (AUD) was identified as the most important modifiable risk factor for early-onset dementia, accounting for 38.9% of 57,353 cases ([Citation11](#)). Similarly, a 2023 report from South Korea also provided supporting evidence, analyzing 100,292 dementia-diagnosed individuals and following up 3,933,382 people over an average of 6.3 years, which showed alcohol consumption of ≥30 g/day was also correlated with an increased risk of dementia ([Citation12](#)).

Epidemiological data from neuropathological and neuroimaging studies support these findings. Postmortem studies in individuals with a history of chronic heavy alcohol use typically report cerebral atrophy and neuronal loss ([Citation13–20](#)). Consistent with these observations, neuroimaging studies in humans following prolonged alcohol use reported reductions in gray and white matter volumes ([Citation21–29](#)). Regionally specific volume reductions have also been reported in alcohol-dependent individuals, and these reductions are associated with cognitive impairment ([Citation25](#), [Citation27](#)). Understand-ing the molecular and cellular mechanisms by which alcohol consumption causes neurodegeneration is essential to elucidating its potential impact on disease onset and progression. Therefore, this review summarizes epidemiological data and clinical evidence on the association between alcohol consumption and major neurodegenerative diseases, as well as mechanistic data from preclinical models.

## Pathophysiological effects of alcohol on the central nervous system

Long-term excessive alcohol consumption is well established to damage the central nervous system (CNS) and is closely associated with alcohol-related neurodegeneration. Chronic alcohol exposure disturbs the balance between inhibitory and excitatory neurotransmission by augmenting γ-aminobutyric acid (GABA) neurotransmission and reducing glutamatergic neurotransmission ([Citation30–32](#)). During chronic alcohol consumption, the compensatory activation of glutamate receptors, particularly N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptors, makes the brain vulnerable to excitotoxic damage during withdrawal. Repeated alcohol withdrawal episodes are therefore associated with withdrawal-induced excessive calcium influx, mitochondrial damage, and activation of the apoptotic pathway, leading to progressive neuronal damage ([Citation33](#), [Citation34](#)). The magnitude and duration of these effects correlate strongly with lifetime alcohol consumption and the duration of chronic alcohol exposure ([Citation35](#)).

Chronic alcohol use predisposes individuals to neurodegenerative diseases, not only through direct neurotoxicity but also indirectly via systemic comorbidities. Long-term excessive alcohol use is associated with an increased risk of hypertension, cardiac arrhythmias, cerebrovascular disease, and metabolic disorders, leading to impaired cerebral blood flow and disruption of the blood-brain barrier (BBB) ([Citation36–39](#)). Clinically, chronic alcohol consumption manifests as cognitive impairment, cerebellar ataxia, gait disturbances, peripheral neuropathy, and alcohol-related neurocognitive disorders, including Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome. Importantly, alcohol-related brain dysfunction is not limited to neuronal death but also includes widespread disruption of synaptic connections and neuronal network function, thus impairing the performance of the remaining neurons ([Citation40–43](#)). Furthermore, chronic heavy alcohol consumption decreases levels of neurotrophic factors, such as brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and nerve growth factor (NGF) ([Citation44](#)), which are essential for neuronal survival, growth, differentiation, and maintenance. It also promotes microglia- and astrocyte-mediated neuroinflammatory responsesand induces gut microbiota dysbiosis, resulting in increased intestinal permeability and the translocation of endotoxins across the BBB to the brain. Collectively, these interconnected mechanisms-including oxidative stress, neuroinflammation, and gut-brain axis dysfunction-highlight the neurotoxic effects of chronic alcohol exposure and its important role in the pathophysiology of neurodegenerative diseases ().

### Oxidative stress

Neuronal integrity is maintained by a finely regulated balance between the production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and the body’s antioxidant defense system ([Citation45](#)). Chronic alcohol consumption disrupts this balance, primarily through the sustained induction of cytochrome P450 2E1 (CYP2E1), a key enzyme in ethanol metabolism, which produces excessive amounts of ROS during prolonged exposure. The resulting oxidative stress promotes lipid peroxidation, protein oxidation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and the activation of apoptotic pathways, thereby increasing the vulnerability of neurons to damage.

Experimental evidence indicates that modulation of CYP2E1 activity or downstream oxidative pathways can attenuate alcohol-induced neurotoxicity, supporting a mechanistic link between ethanol metabolism and oxidative neuronal damage ([Citation46](#)). Consistent with these findings, experimental and clinical studies have reported associations between alcohol-related oxidative stress, neuronal apoptosis, and impairments in cognitive function ([Citation47](#)). At the same time, chronic alcohol consumption impairs gastrointestinal function and nutrient absorption, leading to depletions in antioxidant vitamins that further increase the alcoholic neuropathies’ oxidative burden and may contribute to endothelial vasoconstriction, decreased cerebral perfusion, and increased susceptibility to ischemic and hypoxic brain injury ([Citation48](#)). Finally, oxidative stress is closely associated with neuroinflammatory processes. Endogenously produced alcohol-induced ROS can activate microglia and astrocytes to produce pro-inflammatory cytokines, including interleukin-1β (IL-1β) and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), thereby enhancing the positive feedback loop of oxidative and inflammatory damage related to the progression of neurodegenerative diseases.

### Neuroinflammation

Chronic alcohol consumption induces robust neuroimmune signaling in the CNS through prolonged microglial and astrocyte activation, which results in the amplification of pro-inflammatory cascades that damage neuronal homeostasis and survival ([Citation49](#)).

Preclinical studies consistently show that alcohol consumption, both chronic and intermittent, can induce sustained microglial activation specific to certain brain regions. Alcohol has been shown to induce microglial activation in brain regions involved in cognition and motor control, such as the hippocampus, frontal cortex, amygdala, and substantia nigra. Importantly, increased expression of several microglial activation markers, including ionized calcium-binding adaptor molecule 1 (Iba1) and 18 kDa translocator protein (TSPO), persists for several weeks after alcohol withdrawal, suggesting that alcohol exposure induces a long-lasting shift in the brain’s immune environment rather than an acute inflammatory state ([Citation50](#), [Citation51](#)). Long-term studies of voluntary alcohol consumption also show the persistence of proinflammatory microglial phenotypes in the hippocampus, characterized by increased cytokine expression ([Citation52](#), [Citation53](#)).

At the molecular level, high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) has recently been recognized as an important mediator of alcohol-induced activation of the brain’s innate immune system. Alcohol exposure induces translocation of HMGB1 from the nucleus to the cytoplasm and its extracellular release, where it acts as a damage-associated molecular pattern (DAMP). Extracellular HMGB1 interacts with pattern recognition receptors, particularly the Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) and the receptor for advanced glycation end products (RAGE), to drive downstream activation of NF-κB dependent transcriptional programs and sustained production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, including TNF-α and IL-1β ([Citation54](#), [Citation55](#)). HMGB1 signaling is also implicated in tau pathology and dopaminergic neuronal vulnerability, linking alcohol-induced innate immune activation to disease-associated neurodegenerative processes ([Citation54](#), [Citation55](#)). Furthermore, alcohol-induced neuroinflammation is enhanced by positive feedback loops involving HMGB1 and other inflammatory mediators. HMGB1 forms functional complexes with lipopolysaccharide (LPS), IL-1β, and the chemokine ligand CXC 12 (CXCL12), which enhances immune receptor activation and prolongs inflammatory signaling pathways ([Citation56](#)). These mechanisms may be particularly important in the context of alcohol-induced intestinal barrier dysfunction and endotoxemia, enhancing central immune activation through peripheral and central immune crosstalk. Other experimental studies have shown that alcohol-induced brain endothelial cell injury leads to the release of extracellular ATP, which activates the purinergic receptor P2×7 (P2X7R) to drive BBB disruption and secondary microglial activation ([Citation57](#)).

Although animal studies have identified several alcohol-activated molecular pathways that trigger neuroinflammation, human data are more limited but generally consistent. Clinically, chronic alcohol consumption has been associated with both systemic and central inflammatory abnormalities. To date, elevated concentrations of proinflammatory cytokines, including TNF-α and interleukin-6 (IL-6), have been reported in individuals with AUD, with some reports suggesting that these alterations persist even after long-term abstinence ([Citation58](#)). Furthermore, postmortem studies of human brains have shown increased microglial activation and overexpression of proinflammatory mediators in the prefrontal cortex of individuals with AUD ([Citation59](#)). Consistent with these findings, meta-analytic studies have demonstrated that alcohol-dependent individuals have increased levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines in the circulation compared with healthy controls ([Citation60](#)).

### Gut microbiota dysbiosis

The gut microbiome influences neuronal development, immune homeostasis, and neuronal function through bidirectional signaling along the gut-brain axis. Preclinical studies have shown that alcohol consumption leads to alterations in the composition and function of the gut microbiota and disrupts gut-brain communication ([Citation61](#)). Such alterations are often associated with disruption of the intestinal barrier, allowing microbial products (including endotoxins) to enter the systemic circulation and potentially exacerbate peripheral inflammatory responses ([Citation62](#)).

Preclinical studies suggest that increased intestinal permeability and systemic inflammation can disrupt the BBB and facilitate the entry of inflammatory mediators into the CNS. These endotoxins activate Toll-like receptor-dependent signaling in microglia and astrocytes, promote NF-κB-mediated inflammatory cascades, and increase expression of glial activation markers such as Iba1 and glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) ([Citation63–65](#)). Furthermore, alcohol-related gut dysbiosis and alterations in neurotransmitter balance may have adverse effects on synaptic plasticity and neuronal survival ([Citation66](#), [Citation67](#)). Notably, alcohol itself has been shown to impair BBB integrity, thereby promoting neuroimmune crosstalk and increasing the central vulnerability to systemic inflammatory signals.

Several pieces of clinical evidence support the relevance of this pathway in individuals with AUD. Notably, specific patterns of gut microbiota dysbiosis have been associated with increased circulating inflammatory mediators, reduced white matter integrity, and greater withdrawal symptom severity ([Citation68](#), [Citation69](#)). Importantly, periods of alcohol abstinence have been shown to partially restore gut barrier function and reduce systemic inflammation, accompanied by improvements in cognitive and affective measures ([Citation70](#)). Nonetheless, the available clinical evidence is observational in nature and longitudinal studies have yet to directly link alterations in the gut microbiota to the onset or progression of neurodegenerative disease.

### Alzheimer’s disease (AD)

AD is a progressive neurodegenerative disease characterized by a gradual decline in memory and cognitive abilities. The major pathological features of AD have been demonstrated to be aggregation of extracellular amyloid β-protein (Aβ) plaques and hyperphosphorylation of intracellular tau protein. Recent studies have demonstrated that alcohol consumption modulates the onset and progression of AD through multiple interrelated pathways. These mechanisms include impaired amyloid precursor protein (APP)/Aβ metabolism, abnormal tau phosphorylation, chronic neuroinflammation, impaired neurotrophic support, and disruption of the gut-brain axis.

#### Epidemiological evidence of alcohol and AD risk

Epidemiological studies investigating the relationship between alcohol consumption and the risk of developing AD are heterogeneous and sometimes contradictory. Large population-based studies have shown that chronic alcohol use (>21 units/week) and alcohol dependence are associated with an increased risk of all forms of dementia and AD, as well as more rapid cognitive decline ([Citation25](#), [Citation71](#)). Conversely, several prospective cohort studies report positive effects of alcohol consumption on cognitive performance. For example, in a German cohort study followed for three years, all participants that reported one drink per week to four drinks per day had a lower risk of developing all forms of dementia (HR = 0.71, *p*  =.028) and AD (HR = 0.58, *p*  =.013) than abstainers ([Citation72](#)). Likewise, a U-shaped relationship between alcohol intake and cognitive performance was observed in a U.S. cohort of 19,887 middle-aged and older adults, with peak performance at very low levels of consumption ([Citation73](#)). From a public health perspective, alcohol consumption patterns characterized by chronic or episodic binge drinking are far more common and are strongly associated with negative cognitive consequences.

#### Aβ and tau pathology in alcohol-related AD progression

Aβ and tau play a central role in the progression of alcohol-induced AD. Experimental studies show that ethanol modulates the molecular pathology of AD in a concentration- and time-dependent manner. Low ethanol concentrations (0.02–0.08%) can protect against Aβ-induced synaptic toxicity and neuronal death and induce transient neuronal survival. Conversely, higher ethanol concentrations and prolonged exposure chronically increase the expression of APP, BACE1, and Aβ, promoting amyloid plaque formation and cognitive impairment ([Citation74](#), [Citation75](#)). Ethanol also significantly enhances tau pathology. In 3xTg-AD mice, even short-term alcohol exposure (one month) increases tau phosphorylation at GSK-3β target sites in the hippocampus ([Citation76](#)). Chronic alcohol exposure leads to increased GSK-3β activity and induces cyclin-dependent kinase 5 (Cdk5) activation via calpain-induced p25 proteolytic processing, thereby facilitating alcohol-induced tau hyperphosphorylation, microtubule destabilization, and neuronal dysfunction ([Citation77–81](#)). Consistent with these observations, GSK-3β and caspase-3 activation in fetal alcohol spectrum disorder models leads to tau degradation and neuronal apoptosis ([Citation82](#)). Collectively, these preclinical studies support the hypothesis that altered Aβ-tau signaling is a mechanism by which alcohol may exacerbate AD-like pathology.

#### Neuroinflammation plays a central role in alcohol-related AD progression

Neuroinflammation is increasingly recognized as a key factor in the pathophysiology of AD, and recent evidence suggests that it is exacerbated by chronic alcohol consumption ([Citation83–86](#)). Ethanol and its metabolized product acetaldehyde possess direct neurotoxic properties but also modulate properties of immunomodulatory agents in a dose- and exposure- dependent manner ([Citation87–89](#)). Chronic alcohol consumption can disrupt immune homeostasis and promote a proinflammatory environment that can persist during the disease course. At the molecular level, alcohol can upregulate innate immune signaling pathways, including TLR4 and HMGB1, thereby activating NF-κB-dependent inflammatory cascades ([Citation55](#)). Concurrently, it suppresses cAMP response element-binding protein (CREB)-dependent neurotrophic signaling ([Citation90](#), [Citation91](#)), thereby exacerbating chronic inflammation and neuronal vulnerability. Genomic studies demonstrate the importance of neuroimmunological mechanisms in the pathogenesis of AD, with several AD susceptibility loci identified in a Genome-wide association study (GWAS) implicated in immune regulation ([Citation92](#)). Experimental studies have demonstrated that immune activation precedes and potentiates tau pathology. Thus, in P301S transgenic mice, microglial activation precedes neurofibrillary tangle (NFT) formation, and LPS stimulation enhances the activity of tau kinases Cdk5 and GSK-3β ([Citation93–97](#)). In 3×Tg-AD mice, pharmacological blockade of the IL-1 receptor attenuates the activation of Cdk5/p25, GSK-3β, and p38 MAPK, and mitigates tau hyperphosphorylation and cognition ([Citation93](#), [Citation98](#), [Citation99](#)). Transcriptomic analyses have further demonstrated that ethanol exposure disrupts microglial phagocytic gene networks, which can impair Aβ clearance and increase amyloid burden ([Citation100](#)). Furthermore, alcohol can shift astrocyte polarization toward the proinflammatory A1 phenotype and induce extracellular cold-inducible RNA-binding protein (eCIRP) signaling, leading to global oxidative stress, cytokine release, and tau pathology ([Citation101–103](#)).

#### Neurotrophic factor pathways in alcohol-related AD progression

Neurotrophic factors are essential for neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity, and neurogenesis. Emerging evidence has shown that the consumption of alcohol can inhibit the expression and signaling of several neurotrophic molecules, disrupt neuronal environment, and induce neuronal apoptosis associated with AD. Notably, BDNF plays a key role in AD due to its close association with cognitive impairment and neurodegeneration. Chronic alcohol consumption results in the inhibition of BDNF expression, which antagonizes the neuroprotective effects of BDNF and enhances the neuropathology of AD ([Citation44](#)). BDNF mainly activates tropomyosin receptor kinase B (TrkB), which in turn activates the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase/protein kinase B (PI3K/Akt) and mitogen-activated protein kinase/extracellular signal-regulated kinase (MAPK/ERK) signaling cascades, thereby conferring neuronal survival and the maintenance of synaptic connections. Alcohol reduces BDNF, which attenuates these pathways and enhances the susceptibility to oxidative stress, inflammation, and synaptic dysfunction. In addition to BDNF, alcohol also suppresses the expression of other neurotrophic factors. For instance, insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) plays a role in the regulation of Aβ clearance and tau protein phosphorylation. However, chronic alcohol consumption results in a great reduction in both circulating and central IGF-1 levels, thereby facilitating Aβ deposition, tau dysregulation and cognitive decline ([Citation104](#), [Citation105](#)). Furthermore, alcohol decreases the expression of glial cell-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF), which may further reduce neuronal resilience in the aging brain ([Citation106](#)). Clinical studies have shown that individuals with alcohol dependence have reduced serum and cerebrospinal fluid levels of BDNF and that this reduction correlates with cognitive decline ([Citation107](#)). Finally, alcohol consumption leads to a decrease in circulating IGF-1 levels ([Citation108](#)). Taken together, these studies suggest that depletion of neurotrophic factors may be a likely mechanism in the transition from alcohol exposure to neurodegeneration associated with AD and that restoring neurotrophic signaling may offer a therapeutic option ([Citation109](#), [Citation110](#)).

#### Gut microbiota-brain axis in alcohol-related AD progression

The intestinal microbiota is essential for maintaining neuronal and immunological homeostasis. Chronic alcohol consumption leads to persistent changes in the composition of the intestinal microbiota, weakens the intestinal barrier, and may contribute to the development of AD pathology via the gut-brain axis ([Citation111](#)). Clinical studies in patients with AD report decreased microbial diversity and a predominance of proinflammatory taxa, accompanied by a decrease in anti-inflammatory species and an increase in species such as Escherichia, Shigella, and Clostridiaceae ([Citation112–114](#)). Alcohol-induced dysbiosis leads to increased intestinal permeability and the translocation of LPS and other microbial products into the bloodstream. These factors can disrupt the integrity of the BBB, activate pattern recognition receptors on microglia and neurons, and promote the expression of inflammatory genes via the NF-κB inflammatory cascade, thereby facilitating neuronal injury ([Citation63](#), [Citation64](#), [Citation115–118](#)). Changes in microbial metabolites, including short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), bile acids, and tryptophan derivatives, can modulate Aβ accumulation, tau pathology, and neurodegenerative processes ([Citation119–121](#)). These studies provide compelling evidence for the importance of the gut-brain axis in the progression of alcohol-related AD; however, in humans, this is primarily associative, and longitudinal studies establishing a link between the onset and progression of AD and alcohol-related changes in the gut microbiota are needed.

### Parkinson’s disease (PD)

PD is a chronic and progressive neurodegenerative disorder that is clinically diagnosed by bradykinesia, resting tremor, rigidity, and postural instability and pathologically defined by the selective degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta and the accumulation of α-synuclein (αSYN)-containing Lewy bodies ([Citation122](#)). Genetic mutations lead to a familial form of PD; however, most cases occur sporadically, suggesting the existence of genetic modifiers, including environmental factors such as alcohol consumption, that may influence susceptibility to and progression of PD.

#### Epidemiological evidence linking alcohol consumption and PD risk

Population-based studies examining the association between alcohol consumption and the risk of PD have yielded conflicting results. Large-scale cohort studies more consistently show that chronic heavy alcohol consumption, or alcohol dependence, is associated with an increased risk of PD. A 13-year Swedish national cohort study involving 602,930 individuals found that alcohol dependence was associated with a significantly increased risk of PD (HR = 1.38, *p*  <.001), suggesting a detrimental effect of chronic heavy alcohol consumption on the dopaminergic system ([Citation123](#)). In contrast, some observational studies have reported an inverse association between alcohol consumption below or within the range of current moderate drinking guidelines (<8 drinks per week for women and <15 drinks per week for men), particularly beer consumption, and the risk of PD, predominantly in men ([Citation124–126](#)). Possible mechanisms underlying these associations may include modulation of uric acid metabolism and antioxidant effects. However, these associations have not been consistently replicated in other cohorts, were not observed in women, and are highly susceptible to residual confounding, selection bias, and lifetime alcohol consumption misclassification. Moreover, some studies have reported that spirits consumption may be associated with faster progression to advanced PD stages, possibly through mechanisms involving oxidative stress and neuroinflammation, with limited direct mechanistic evidence in humans ([Citation127](#)). Thus, available epidemiological data do not support a clinically significant protective effect of alcohol in PD, while heavy and chronic alcohol consumption is more consistently associated with increased risk or severity of the disease.

#### Preclinical evidence linking alcohol exposure to dopaminergic vulnerability

In contrast to the conflicting results of human studies, preclinical models have more consistently demonstrated dopaminergic neurotoxicity following chronic exposure to high doses of alcohol. Animal studies have shown that chronic ethanol administration lowers dopamine (DA) levels in the brain, inhibits dopaminergic neurotransmission, and promotes the expression and aggregation of αSYN, a pathological marker of PD ([Citation123](#), [Citation127](#), [Citation128](#)). SNCA encodes αSYN and is important for both familial and sporadic PD, as well as alcohol-related neurobehavioral phenotypes. Chronic ethanol exposure in alcohol-dependent rat models results in increased αSYN levels and blunted DA release in the nucleus accumbens, implying that alcohol-induced neuroadaptations involve αSYN-mediated dopaminergic dysfunction ([Citation129](#)). Ethanol-induced oxidative stress would also enhance chronic ethanol-induced αSYN accumulation and facilitate the associated dopaminergic neuronal damages ([Citation130](#)). Aggregated αSYN induces presynaptic dopaminergic neurotoxicity by disrupting the vesicular monoamine transporter 2 (VMAT2)-dependent sequestration and synaptic release of DA ([Citation131–133](#)). Chronic ethanol exposure also induces electrophysiological and synaptic alterations in the dopaminergic circuit. Chronic ethanol exposure causes a reduction in the current density of I <sub><i>h</i></sub>, a component of the GABA-gated I <sub><i>h</i></sub> current, which results in a decreased number of firings of ventral tegmental area dopaminergic neurons during withdrawal ([Citation134](#), [Citation135](#)). Furthermore, sensitization of dopamine D2 autoreceptors reduces DA release in monkeys and mice ([Citation136](#), [Citation137](#)). Consistently, chronic ethanol exposure in mice results in decreased DA release and increased DA uptake in dopaminergic terminals, which improves presynaptic regulation ([Citation137](#)).

Although acute ethanol exposure causes an increase in DA release, chronic alcohol consumption would lead to dopaminergic depletion in the nigrostriatal pathway in the chronic alcohol model, probably partially recapitulating the dopaminergic neurochemical phenotype observed in PD ([Citation123](#)). VMAT2 expression is increased at the onset of chronic alcohol exposure but decreases in the caudate nucleus and putamen with prolonged exposure, indicating a progressive impairment of DA processing ([Citation138](#)). Furthermore, in toxin-induced PD models, such as 1-methyl-4-phenyl-1,2,3,6-tetrahydropyridine (MPTP)-treated mice, chronic alcohol consumption enhances MPTP-induced dopaminergic neurodegeneration and increases DA loss and lipid peroxidation in the striatum, thereby exacerbating neurotoxic lesions ([Citation139](#)). The available results therefore suggest that chronic and heavy alcohol consumption in animal models leads to dopaminergic system dysfunction, αSYN pathology and oxidative stress.

### Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (Also)

Also is a fatal neurodegenerative disease characterized by progressive weakness of limbs, dysphagia, dysarthria, and eventual respiratory failure ([Citation140–142](#)). The disease usually develops between the ages of 55 and 75 and is more common in men ([Citation143](#), [Citation144](#)). The development of this disease may be the result of the interaction between genetic factors and environment.

#### Epidemiological evidence linking alcohol consumption and Also risk

Epidemiological studies on the association between alcohol consumption and the risk of developing Also have yielded conflicting results. Some case-control and cohort studies have reported inverse associations between alcohol consumption and Also risk. For instance, former and current alcohol drinkers had a significantly lower risk of developing Also than former drinkers (OR = 0.67, 95% CI: 0.40–1.13) and current drinkers (OR = 0.52, 95% CI: 0.40–0.75) ([Citation145](#)). Similarly, a nationwide cohort study of 7,965 individuals diagnosed with AUD between 1973 and 2010 reported a significantly lower standardized incidence ratio for Also (SIR = 0.54, 95% CI: 0.45–0.63) ([Citation146](#)). On the other hand, no association between alcohol consumption and Also risk was observed in Japanese ([Citation147](#)) and New England cohorts (OR = 1.10) ([Citation148](#)). The Euro-MOTOR study also found no association between alcohol or red wine consumption and Also incidence (OR = 0.93, 95% CI: 0.75–1.15) ([Citation149](#)). Notably, Mendelian randomization analyses have found that genetically determined alcohol consumption is associated with an increased risk of Also; each 10 g/day increase in alcohol consumption was associated with an approximately 1.5-fold increase in Also risk (95% CI: 1.4–3.4) ([Citation150](#)). Overall, the results of these studies indicate a lack of consensus regarding the influence of alcohol on Also susceptibility.

#### Preclinical evidence exploring alcohol-related mechanisms in Also

Mechanistic understanding of the effects of alcohol on Also pathogenesis is largely based on preclinical studies in animal models. Depending on the dose, duration of exposure and relevant molecular phenotype, alcohol appears to have protective effect. In particular, various antioxidant components found in alcoholic beverages, such as polyphenols, have protected neurons against glutamate-induced oxidative stress and neuronal apoptosis in vitro and in transgenic Also models of *superoxide dismutase-1* (*SOD1*) and *chromosome 9 open reading frame 72* (*C9orf72*) mutations ([Citation151–155](#)), with modest success in neuronal survival. Conversely, chronic alcohol consumption induces maladaptive changes in glutamatergic neurotransmission and increased excitotoxicity, a key pathogenic mechanism of Also. Chronic ethanol exposure increases extracellular glutamate and impairs glutamate transporter function, thereby enhancing excitotoxic motor neuron death. Given these overlapping mechanisms, it has been hypothesized that pharmacological agents targeting glutamate homeostasis and neurotransmission, such as ceftriaxone, which upregulates glutamate transporter expression and has been investigated for potential therapeutic benefit in both AUD and Also ([Citation156](#)). Collectively, the preclinical studies suggest that the impact of alcohol on relevant Also pathways is complex and exposure-specific. While several antioxidant components found in alcoholic beverages can mitigate oxidative or excitotoxic damage in animal models, chronic or high-dose alcohol exposure more consistently leads to increased glutamatergic excitotoxicity and heightened motor neuron vulnerability. It is important to note that these mechanistic observations do not imply protection against Also conferred by alcohol consumption in humans and have not been confirmed in Also patients.

### Huntington’s disease (HD)

HD is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by motor dysfunction, mental impairment, and cognitive decline, ultimately leading to dementia ([Citation157](#)). It is caused by an expansion of the CAG trinucleotide sequence in the huntingtin (*HTT*) gene, resulting in the expression of mutated HTT protein and neuronal dysfunction, primarily affecting the striatum and cerebral cortex. The genetic identity of HD is clear and strongly correlated with disease onset; however, the influence of environmental and lifestyle factors on the phenotypic manifestation or course of the disease remains to be elucidated.

#### Epidemiological evidence linking alcohol consumption and HD

Epidemiological studies examining the relationship between alcohol consumption and HD have produced inconsistent findings. Some reports suggest higher rates of alcohol use among individuals (≥14 units per week) with HD compared with the general population ([Citation158](#)), whereas others, including case-control analyses, indicate comparable prevalence rates. Longitudinal observations have shown that alcohol use can precede the onset of clinical symptoms of HD in some individuals and is often associated with depressive symptoms, impulsivity, and other psychiatric manifestations inherent to the disease, complicating causal interpretation. Several observational studies have reported associations between alcohol consumption and earlier age at disease onset. However, Mendelian randomization analyses do not confirm a causal relationship between alcohol consumption and the onset or progression of HD ([Citation158](#), [Citation159](#)). Large-scale observational data from the Enroll-HD cohort also indicate that the phenotypic variability of Huntington’s disease reflects complex interactions between genetic predisposition and environmental factors, while the independent contribution of alcohol exposure remains uncertain ([Citation160](#)). Currently, there is no clear evidence that alcohol consumption represents a definitive risk factor or disease modifier in HD.

#### Clinical and preclinical insights into alcohol-HD interactions

Clinically, both acute intoxication and chronic alcohol consumption can exacerbate motor dysfunction, cognitive impairment, and psychiatric symptoms in individuals with HD, thereby increasing functional disability and caregiver burden. Alcohol-related neurological disorders such as Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome can further complicate clinical assessment and worsen the overall burden of the disease on affected patients ([Citation161](#)). These effects likely reflect additive or overlapping neurotoxic and behavioral consequences rather than specific pathological mechanisms of HD.

Mechanistic evidence regarding alcohol-HD interactions is obtained almost entirely from animal models. HTT protein is involved in neuroprotective processes, while mutated huntingtin exhibits toxic gain-of-function properties that contribute to the pathogenesis of HD. Experimental studies in *Drosophila* have shown that loss of *dhtt* alters sensitivity and tolerance to ethanol, suggesting a potential role for huntingtin in modulating neuronal responses to alcohol exposure ([Citation162](#)).

## Conclusions

Across the neurodegenerative disorders discussed, chronic heavy alcohol consumption increases vulnerability through multiple neurobiological mechanisms. Neurodegenerative disorders we mentioned, chronic heavy alcohol consumption leads to disease vulnerability through multiple neurobiological mechanisms, including perturbations of protein homeostasis, neurotransmitter balance, neuroimmune regulation, and neuronal survival, mediated by shared pathogenic mechanisms such as oxidative stress, persistent neuroinflammation, glial dysfunction, and dysregulation of the gut-brain axis, which impair neural integrity and resilience.

Despite the possibility that low levels of alcohol consumption may be associated with neutral or even positive associations with neuronal outcomes, these associations are often weak and likely confounded by residual factors related to socioeconomic status, cardiovascular disease, or other lifestyle factors. Importantly, such exposure patterns are rare in real-world populations, whereas heavy drinking and binge drinking are common and associated with cognitive impairment and neurodegenerative vulnerability. From a clinical and public health perspective, the weight of evidence suggests a net detrimental effect of alcohol on long-term brain health.

In summary, the findings reviewed here support the need to treat alcohol consumption as a modifiable risk factor in efforts to prevent or mitigate neurodegenerative disease. Future work integrating longitudinal clinical cohort studies, more refined assessments of exposure, and mechanistically relevant biomarkers will be needed to clarify dose-response relationships and to guide targeted prevention and intervention.
